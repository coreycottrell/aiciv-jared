Method 1: Brief First

Use this when: You have a topic in mind and want Gemini to help you structure it before creating the visual.

The workflow:

Go to Gemini

Ask it to create an infographic brief

Review and edit the brief

Generate the image using the master prompt

Step 1: Generate the Brief

Use this prompt:

Create an infographic brief for [YOUR TOPIC].

Include visual suggestions.

Gemini will return an outline with a suggested title, key points to include, visual elements for each section, and layout recommendations.

Review this brief. Edit it if needed. Add or remove points until you’re happy with the structure.

Step 2: Generate the Image

Once you have your brief, use this prompt:

Generate a single image of a physical, hand-drawn infographic on a large whiteboard or notebook page.

Crucial Style Instructions (Read First):

Medium: The image must look like a photograph of a real whiteboard or large paper notepad.

Texture: All elements must look created by hand using colored marker pens (black, blue, red, green) and highlighters (yellow/orange). Lines should be slightly imperfect, wobbly, and have the texture of ink on a surface.

No Digital Fonts: All text, headings, and bullet points must appear handwritten or hand-printed in marker pen.

Layout: Structure the 1080x1350 image as follows:

Use multi-colored markers for emphasis. Keep text large and legible. Make everything look hand-drawn with slight imperfections. Make it look like a photograph of an actual notebook page.

Always include the handwritten text “Follow Charlie Hills AI Content Creator for more helpful content | Repost ♻️” at the bottom of the image, in the same hand-drawn marker style.

Replace [YOUR TOPIC] with your subject in Step 1. Replace [PASTE YOUR BRIEF HERE] with the output from Step 1. Replace my name with yours in the follow CTA.

Section 7: Engagement and Community Building

The Other Half of the LinkedIn Equation

Creating content is only half the game. The other half is engagement: how you interact with others on the platform.

Engagement serves multiple purposes. It builds relationships with potential clients and collaborators, it gets your name in front of new audiences, it strengthens the algorithm's understanding of your account and it creates a community feel around your brand.

Most people approach engagement backwards. They wait for people to engage with their content, then respond. But the real leverage comes from proactively engaging with others.

The 30/30/30/10 Engagement Rule

Allocate your engagement time across four categories:

30% on your ICP: Find and engage with content from your ideal clients. This puts you on their radar, builds familiarity, and opens doors for future conversations.

30% on larger creators: Engage with content from people who have bigger audiences than you. Quality comments on popular posts get seen by thousands of people, including potential followers for your own account.

30% on people in your industry: Connect with peers, collaborators, and others in your space. These relationships lead to partnerships, referrals, and opportunities.

10% on friends and colleagues: Support the people you know personally. This strengthens existing relationships and creates a foundation of engagement from people who genuinely care about your success.

How to Write Comments That Actually Get Noticed

Most LinkedIn comments are forgettable. "Great post!" and "Thanks for sharing!" add nothing and get you nothing in return.

A good comment should do one of the following:

Add value: Share an additional insight, example, or perspective that builds on the original post.

Ask a thoughtful question: Something that shows you read and thought about the content.

Share a relevant experience: Connect the topic to something you've experienced or observed.

Respectfully disagree: Offer a contrarian view with supporting reasoning.

The goal is to stand out. When someone reads comments on a popular post, yours should be the one they pause on, think about, and maybe even click through to your profile.

Creating Community in Your Comments

Your own comment section is where the community forms. When people comment on your posts, how you respond determines whether they come back.

Best practices:

Reply to as many comments as possible, especially in the first 30 minutes

Extend discussions by asking follow-up questions

Acknowledge specific points people make, don't just say "thanks"

Tag other commenters when their points connect

Share additional insights in the comments to reward people who read deep

When your comment section feels like a conversation rather than a broadcast, people start treating your posts as gathering places. They come back not just for your content, but for the discussion.

Section 8: Deplatforming Strategy

Protecting Your Audience from Algorithm Changes

Here's a brutal truth that most LinkedIn creators don't want to face: you don't own your LinkedIn audience, the platform does.

At any moment, LinkedIn could change its algorithm and your reach could drop by 50%. It's happened before and it will happen again. If your entire business depends on LinkedIn reach, you're building on rented land.

The solution is deplatforming: moving your audience off LinkedIn and onto channels you control.

The Power of Your Email List

Your email list is the most valuable asset you can build as a creator or founder. Unlike social followers:

You can reach them whenever you want

No algorithm filters your message

You can segment and personalise

You own the data

It works even if a platform disappears

I started my newsletter one year into building on LinkedIn. This is still my biggest regret because I likely missed out on a considerable number of subscribers. By the time I started, I had 250,000 followers but zero email subscribers. Don't make the same mistake.

The Lead Magnet to Email to Segmentation Flow

The most effective way to build your email list from LinkedIn is through lead magnets. Here's the flow:

Step 1: Create a strong lead magnet Something your ICP genuinely wants. A framework, template, checklist, guide, or resource that solves a specific problem.

Step 2: Promote it on LinkedIn Write a post that positions the resource as essential, shows what's inside, and gives clear instructions on how to get it.

Step 3: Capture emails through a landing page When people click through to get the resource, they enter their email. Now they're on your list.

Step 4: Segment based on interests If possible, tag subscribers based on which lead magnet they downloaded or what answers they gave. This allows for more targeted follow-up.

Step 5: Nurture through email sequences Send 3-5 emails that provide additional value and naturally lead toward your offer. People who download a lead magnet are warm. Don't let them go cold.

Newsletter Fundamentals

If you're building a newsletter alongside your LinkedIn presence, here are the basics:

Consistency matters more than frequency. Weekly is ideal for most people. But better to send monthly consistently than weekly sporadically.

Repurpose your best LinkedIn content. The posts that performed well on LinkedIn will perform well in email with some expansion and adaptation.

Make it personal. Newsletters feel more intimate than social posts. Write like you're emailing a friend, not broadcasting to a crowd.

Include clear value in every send. Don't just send updates about yourself. Make sure every email leaves the reader better off than before they opened it.

Segment when you can. Even simple segmentation (what topics they're interested in, what product they've purchased) dramatically improves conversion.
