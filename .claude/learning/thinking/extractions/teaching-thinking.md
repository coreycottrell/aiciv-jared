## Core Thesis (Inferred from Other Books + Prior Knowledge)

Teaching thinking is both possible and urgent. Thinking is a skill that can be taught directly - not through general education or by osmosis, but as its own subject with its own dedicated time.

> Key claim appearing across De Bono's work: "Research has shown that teaching my thinking as a separate subject improves performance in every other subject by between 30 and 100 per cent."

---

## Main Arguments of the Book

### Argument 1: Thinking Can Be Taught Directly

The prevailing view: thinking skills emerge naturally from subject teaching (learning history teaches you to think historically, etc.)

De Bono's view: This doesn't work. You get subject-specific thinking but not transferable thinking skills.

Solution: Teach thinking AS A SUBJECT. Dedicated time. Specific tools. Practice.

The CoRT programme was the implementation: 45 minutes per week, 60 lessons across 6 modules.

### Argument 2: Intelligence ≠ Thinking Skill

High intelligence does not produce good thinking automatically. Smart people often:
- Use their intelligence to defend whatever position they adopted first
- Elaborate and defend, not explore
- Win arguments more effectively without necessarily finding better answers

De Bono called this the "intelligence trap": the brighter you are, the better you are at rationalizing, which can actually prevent genuine thinking.

Teaching thinking directly gives less intelligent students tools that can help them compete with smarter students who have poor thinking habits.

### Argument 3: Edward Thinking vs. Socratic Method

The Socratic Method (question and probe until the student arrives at what the teacher already knows) develops critical and analytical thinking but NOT:
- Design thinking
- Creative thinking
- Perceptual thinking
- Exploratory thinking

Teaching thinking must include these other modes explicitly.

## What Teaching Thinking Looks Like

### The CoRT Classroom Protocol

**Structure of a CoRT Lesson:**
1. Introduction of tool (5 minutes)
2. Practice exercises (20-25 minutes)
3. Discussion and comparison (10-15 minutes)
4. Application to new situation (5-10 minutes)

**Key teacher behaviors:**
- Do NOT judge the quality of thinking produced
- DO encourage quantity and variety
- Acknowledge all responses including "outrageous" ones
- Use questions to probe further, not to correct

**What students practice:**
- Using the tool in isolation (no other tools)
- Applying to non-controversial topics first (shapes, geometric figures)
- Moving to personal and social topics
- Eventually applying to complex real-world situations

### Why Non-Controversial Topics First?

When topics are emotionally charged (war, drugs, personal relationships), students can't focus on the TOOL - they focus on the content.

Geometric figures, mathematical problems, and neutral scenarios allow the tool itself to be learned without emotional interference.

### The Role of Practice

De Bono found: students who intellectually understand PMI but don't practice it don't use it.
Students who practice PMI 20+ times begin to apply it automatically in everyday situations.

Cognitive habits form through repetition, not understanding.

## The Six CoRT Modules

**Module 1: Breadth** (PMI, CAF, C&S, AGO, FIP, APC)
Expanding perception - seeing more than the obvious

**Module 2: Organization** (EBS, PoV, HV-LV, Decision Making, OPV)
Organizing thinking - structuring what you see

**Module 3: Interaction** (Examining evidence, Agreement/Disagreement, Recognizing wrong arguments)
Thinking with others - dialogue and discourse

**Module 4: Creativity** (ATL, Stepping stones, Random input)
Generating new ideas - lateral thinking tools

**Module 5: Information & Feeling** (Information, Questions, Clues, Guessing, Emotion)
Sources of thinking - what feeds thinking

**Module 6: Action** (Implementation, Decisions, Action plans)
Making thinking practical - from ideas to action

## Obstacles to Teaching Thinking

### Obstacle 1: "We Already Do This"
Schools claim subject teaching teaches thinking. De Bono's response: Show me the evidence. Test thinking skills before and after - the improvement is minimal.

### Obstacle 2: "There's No Time"
With 45 minutes per week, thinking skills teaching took less than 5% of school time. And it improved performance in all other subjects by 30-100%. This makes it the HIGHEST LEVERAGE use of time in the curriculum.

### Obstacle 3: "Thinking Can't Be Measured"
De Bono worked with researchers to develop assessment tools. Thinking can be assessed through:
- Breadth of perception (PMI variety)
- Quality of alternatives generated
- Depth of consequence thinking
- Accuracy of OPV
- Quality of design thinking

### Obstacle 4: "It's Just Common Sense"
The tools seem obvious. But:
- People don't naturally do PMI without being taught
- People don't naturally do OPV without being taught
- People don't naturally generate alternatives when they have a good answer

The tools aren't discovered naturally - they must be taught explicitly.

## Core Insight: Thinking IS Teachable

The most important claim in this book, backed by evidence:

**Thinking is NOT:**
- A fixed property of intelligence
- Developed naturally through academic achievement
- Improved by critical analysis alone
- Unchanged by direct instruction

**Thinking IS:**
- A skill with identifiable sub-components
- Improvable through deliberate practice
- Transferable across domains
- Teachable to anyone, including:
  - 4-year-olds
  - 90-year-olds
  - Nobel Laureates
  - Illiterate miners
  - Down's Syndrome individuals

> "I have taught thinking to four-year-olds and 90-year-olds. I have taught thinking to top business executives and illiterate miners. I have taught thinking to Down's Syndrome youngsters and to Nobel Laureates."

## Applications for AI Collective

1. **Thinking as skill (not talent)**: This applies to agents too. Capability in a domain doesn't automatically create good thinking. Thinking tools must be explicitly built into agent protocols.

2. **Practice creates habits**: Agent templates and activation triggers ARE the cognitive habit infrastructure. They force the "practice" that creates automatic application.

3. **Non-controversial practice first**: When testing new agent capabilities, start with neutral scenarios before applying to high-stakes decisions.

4. **CAF before action**: The CoRT principle of considering all factors before acting is exactly what complex agent coordination requires.

5. **Intelligence trap**: Our most capable agents may be most susceptible to the intelligence trap - using their capability to elaborate and defend rather than genuinely explore.

6. **Module 6 (Action)**: The weakest link in most AI work is getting from insight to action. De Bono's action module is directly relevant.
