## Key Patterns Applied

### 1. Human vs AI Task Separation Is a Distinct Content Genre

This document type - "what the human team needs to do" - is different from a strategy doc or a blog post. The audience is a specific person who needs to execute, not read and think. Voice requirements:
- No hedging
- Numbered steps, not paragraphs
- "Exactly what to do" framing
- Explicit time estimates so they can plan their week
- Separate owner callouts (Jared vs Arlene vs either)

### 2. Why Justification Prevents Pushback

The most common failure of task lists is that recipients ask "why do I need to do this?" For tasks that feel like prep work or relationship-building without immediate payoff (journalist engagement, community seeding), including a 1-2 sentence rationale prevents deprioritization.

### 3. Summary Table Is the Most-Used Section

Any task list over 10 items needs a summary table at the end. This is the section people return to after reading once. It should compress: owner, time, and timing into scannable rows.

### 4. Clarifying Questions Section Prevents Execution Blockers

Tasks requiring specific URLs, account access, or decisions that only Jared can make should be surfaced as questions at the end. This prevents Arlene from starting a task and getting stuck mid-execution.
