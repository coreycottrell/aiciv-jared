## GRS Framework Applied (New Pattern for Future Reference)

The GRS framework differs from standard thought leadership posts in three ways:

1. **The Hill**: Every post takes an unambiguous stance. Not "AI is interesting" but "tool mindset is dead, partnership wins." Polarizing = qualifying.

2. **The Enemy**: Name a concept (not a person, not a competitor) that the ideal customer also dislikes. Enemy examples used:
   - "The 'AI as software deployment' model"
   - "SaaS subscription bloat"
   - "The AI vs. humans replacement narrative"
   - "The illusion that using AI = leveraging AI"

3. **The Hook cutoff**: First 2 lines must be incomplete enough to force the "...more" click. Test: would someone scroll past without clicking? If yes, rewrite. Pattern used: short declarative → gap/tension → "...more" needed to resolve.

## Voice Notes (GRS-Specific)

- GRS posts need more conviction than standard posts — "I believe X" not "it seems like X"
- The enemy concept must feel real to the reader — name their frustration, not Jared's critique
- Short sentences create punch (GRS relies on rhythm, not just length)
- The disqualification is built into the angle: if you love collecting AI tools, Post 3 will annoy you. That's correct. It means ideal customers self-select.
