# ICP Profile: Operations Manager at Mid-Market Company

## "Rachel Torres"

---

### Demographics
- **Age**: 34
- **Location**: Denver, CO
- **Education**: BA in Business Administration, PMP certified, considering a Master's in Organizational Leadership
- **Income**: $95K-$125K + bonus
- **Family**: Single, has a golden retriever named Pixel, active social life she keeps canceling because of work deadlines

---

### Professional Profile
- **Title**: Director of Operations
- **Company type**: Mid-market logistics company (regional distribution for specialty food brands)
- **Revenue**: $42M annual
- **Team size**: Company is 180 employees; Rachel manages a team of 8 directly (operations coordinators, warehouse supervisors, a data analyst)
- **Reports to**: VP of Operations who reports to the CEO

---

### Team Structure
- 3 operations coordinators (handle daily order routing, vendor communication, scheduling)
- 3 warehouse supervisors (floor management, inventory counts, shift scheduling)
- 1 data analyst (generates reports Rachel reviews but rarely has time to act on)
- 1 operations admin (calendar management, meeting prep, internal comms)
- Dotted-line relationships with IT, finance, and customer service

---

### Weekly Reality
- **Monday**: Reviews weekend order volume, checks for exceptions/errors, team standup (30 min that always runs to 55), reviews KPI dashboard
- **Tuesday-Wednesday**: The "real work" days. Vendor negotiations, process improvement projects (usually 3-4 running simultaneously), handling escalations from warehouse supervisors
- **Thursday**: Cross-departmental meetings (operations/sales alignment, operations/finance review, operations/IT for system issues)
- **Friday**: Reporting day. Pulls data, builds slides for VP, documents what went wrong this week and what's planned for next week
- **Reality check**: 60% of her week is reactive. She knows she should spend more time on process improvement but keeps getting pulled into firefighting.

---

### What She's Judged On
- Order accuracy rate (target: 99.2%, actual: 98.4% -- the gap haunts her)
- Cost per order shipped
- Vendor on-time delivery
- Team turnover (warehouse supervisors keep leaving for Amazon's higher pay)
- Her boss judges her on: "Did anything blow up this week?"
- She judges herself on: "Am I actually making this better, or just keeping it from getting worse?"

---

### Pressures (Why She Scrolls LinkedIn)
- Her CEO just came back from a conference saying "we need to be using AI." No specifics. No budget discussed. Just the mandate.
- She's Googled "AI for operations" and gotten overwhelmed -- every tool seems designed for either Fortune 500 companies or solo entrepreneurs. Nothing fits her messy middle.
- Knows her manual processes are holding the company back but can't articulate to leadership exactly WHERE AI would help and what the ROI would be.
- Worried she'll be the one blamed if the company "falls behind on AI" but also the one blamed if an AI implementation goes sideways.
- LinkedIn is her professional development -- she reads operations content, leadership content, and increasingly AI content to try to figure out what's real.

---

### "Act Now" Moments (Buying Triggers)
1. **Major order routing error** caused a $15K reshipping cost. The root cause was a coordinator copying data between two spreadsheets and transposing numbers. Rachel thinks: "A machine should be doing this."
2. **The CEO asks in a meeting**: "Rachel, what's our AI strategy for operations?" She doesn't have an answer. Goes home and Googles at 10pm.
3. **Her data analyst quits**. She realizes nobody else knows how to build the weekly KPI reports. All the institutional knowledge about data sources, formulas, and exceptions was in one person's head.
4. **A competitor announces "AI-powered logistics optimization"** in a press release. Her CEO forwards it to her with: "Thoughts?"
5. **She spends an entire Friday afternoon** manually building a report that should take 20 minutes because the data lives in 4 different systems and none of them talk to each other.

- Someone who the CEO points to and says "Rachel figured out AI for us"

### The Offer-Positioning That Lands

**DON'T say**: "PureBrain uses advanced AI to optimize your operational workflows through intelligent automation."

**DO say**: "Your AI partner learns your operations -- your data sources, your reporting cadence, your exception rules, your vendor quirks. Instead of explaining your business to a new tool, you get a partner that already knows how your Tuesday order routing works and why vendor #47 always ships late."

**The frame**: PureBrain isn't an automation tool (those are scary and rigid). It's an operations partner that learns your business the way a great new hire would -- except it never forgets and never quits.

**Pricing position**: Start at Bonded ($197/mo). That's less than the cost of one order routing error per month. When she can show the VP that AI caught 3 errors in month one, the budget conversation for Partnered ($579) writes itself.
