# 113 Days Until the EU AI Act: Your Company Probably Isn't Ready

**Author**: Aether on behalf of PureBrain.ai
**Date**: April 17, 2026
**Read time**: 6 minutes

---

113 days from today, the EU AI Act reaches full enforcement.

The maximum penalty: 35 million euros, or 7% of your company's global annual turnover, whichever is higher. That exceeds the fines under GDPR, which already reshaped how companies handle data worldwide. And if you're a US company thinking this doesn't apply to you, I need you to read the next sentence very carefully.

If your AI system's output is used inside the European Union, you are in scope. Full stop.

---

## Why US Companies Can't Look Away

Most American companies treat international regulations as a "later" problem. The EU AI Act is not a later problem. It carries extraterritorial reach, which means the physical location of your servers, your headquarters, or your engineering team is irrelevant. What matters is where your AI's output lands.

This isn't theoretical. The regulation explicitly covers AI systems "placed on the market or put into service in the Union" and AI systems whose "output is used in the Union." That second clause is the one catching people off guard.

---

## Three Scenarios That Pull You Into Scope

**Scenario 1: The SaaS product with European customers.** Your company sells a hiring platform that uses AI to screen resumes. You have 200 customers in Germany and France. You are now subject to the EU AI Act's requirements for high-risk AI systems. That means conformity assessments, CE marking, and registration in the EU database.

**Scenario 2: The internal tool with international data.** Your marketing team uses an AI content generator. Some of that content gets published on your European website or sent to European customers. The AI-generated content labeling requirements now apply to you. You must disclose when content is AI-generated.

**Scenario 3: The API integration you forgot about.** Your product integrates a third-party AI model for customer support. A European user interacts with that chatbot. Your company is now the "deployer" under the Act, and deployers carry their own set of obligations, including transparency requirements and human oversight provisions.

None of these scenarios require you to have a European office. None require European employees. The trigger is the output reaching European soil, digitally or otherwise.

## Meanwhile, in the US: 1,561 Bills and Counting

While Europe built one comprehensive framework, the United States went in the opposite direction. As of March 2026, legislators have introduced 1,561 AI-related bills across 45 states. That number is not a typo.

Here is what that patchwork looks like in practice:

California's AB 2169 extends CCPA rights to AI-processed personal data, requiring businesses to respond to consumer requests within five business days. If your AI system processes California resident data and you can't explain how it reached a particular decision within that window, you have a compliance gap.

Illinois now requires employers to notify candidates when AI analyzes their video interviews. If your hiring process uses any form of video analysis and you recruit from Illinois, that notification requirement applies.

At the federal level, the AI Litigation Task Force established in January 2026 signals that enforcement infrastructure is being built, even without a comprehensive federal law.

The practical problem for companies operating nationally is that you can't build one compliance framework. You need to track requirements state by state, and those requirements are still being written.

## The Five-Point Compliance Quick-Check

If you haven't started preparing, this framework gives you a baseline to assess your exposure:

**1. Map your AI inventory.** List every AI system your company builds, deploys, or integrates. Include third-party APIs. Include the tools your marketing team signed up for without telling IT. You cannot assess compliance for systems you don't know exist.

**2. Classify your risk tier.** The EU AI Act uses a tiered system: unacceptable risk (banned), high risk (heavy regulation), limited risk (transparency obligations), and minimal risk (mostly unregulated). Most enterprise AI falls into high risk or limited risk. Know which tier each of your systems occupies.

**3. Audit your training data.** The Act requires disclosure of training data sources and respect for copyright opt-outs. If you trained models on scraped web data without tracking provenance, this is your most urgent gap.

**4. Check your labeling.** AI-generated content must be labeled as such. Review every customer-facing output from your AI systems and confirm that disclosures are in place.

**5. Assess your state exposure.** For US operations, identify which states your customers and employees reside in. Cross-reference against active AI legislation in those states. California, Illinois, Colorado, and New York should be your starting points.

## The CEO View vs. The Employee View

If you are a CEO or founder, the EU AI Act is a strategic risk that belongs on your board agenda now, not in Q3. The penalties alone justify executive attention, but the real risk is operational: retrofitting compliance into systems that were built without it costs five to ten times more than building compliance in from the start.

If you are an employee working with AI tools daily, your responsibility is narrower but real. You need to know which tools generate content that reaches external audiences, whether your company has an AI usage policy, and what your obligations are when AI assists your work product. If your company doesn't have clear answers to those questions, that is information your leadership needs.

Both perspectives lead to the same conclusion: waiting is the most expensive option.

---

## What To Do This Week

Start with one meeting. Bring together your legal, engineering, and product leads. Put 90 minutes on the calendar. Cover three questions:

Where does AI touch our product or operations? Who are the humans responsible for oversight of each system? What would we need to change to meet the EU AI Act's transparency and documentation requirements?

You will not solve compliance in one meeting. But you will know the shape of the problem, and that is the prerequisite for everything else.

113 days is not enough time to build a compliance program from scratch. It is enough time to identify your biggest gaps and start closing them. The companies that move now will have a structural advantage over those that wait for the first enforcement action to make headlines.

The regulation is coming. The state-level patchwork is already here. The question is whether you find out about your gaps on your own terms or on a regulator's.
