# AI Created 1.3 Million Jobs. Here's Why Everyone Still Feels Terrified

*The data says one thing. Your gut says another. Both are telling you the truth, just about different things entirely.*

---

## The Numbers Should Be Reassuring

Let's start with what the data actually says.

AI has added 1.3 million jobs globally since the generative AI boom began, according to LinkedIn and World Economic Forum tracking. Total US employment is up roughly 2.5% since ChatGPT launched. BCG's 2026 workforce analysis says AI will reshape far more jobs than it eliminates. And an MIT study published April 2nd directly challenges the "job apocalypse" narrative that has dominated headlines for three years.

So why does nobody feel better?

Because the people reading those headlines are looking at their own teams, their own industries, their own kids graduating college this spring, and seeing something the aggregate numbers don't capture. The feeling isn't irrational. It's picking up on a signal the national employment figures are designed to smooth over.

---

## What the Dallas Fed Actually Found

Here's the finding that should be getting more airtime: the Dallas Federal Reserve published research showing AI simultaneously replaces entry-level workers while complementing experienced ones.

Read that again. Not "replaces some, helps others" across different industries. Within the same organizations, within the same roles, AI is hollowing out the bottom while reinforcing the top.

The evidence shows up clearly in the wage data. In the most AI-exposed industries, overall employment has only dipped about 1%. A small number. But wages in those same sectors are surging. National average weekly wages are up 7.5%. In computer systems design specifically? Up 16.7%.

The people who still have jobs are getting paid significantly more. That's what happens when you remove the bottom rungs and concentrate work among experienced professionals who can direct AI tools effectively.

But here's the number that should concern every executive planning five years out: computer systems design employment is down 5% since ChatGPT launched, even as every other metric in tech keeps climbing. The sector most fluent in AI is already showing the pattern the rest of the economy will follow.

---

## The Career Ladder Problem Nobody Wants to Talk About

Young workers under 25 aren't being fired. That's technically true. What's happening is quieter and harder to measure: they're not being hired in the first place.

Companies that used to bring on junior analysts, associate designers, entry-level developers to train over three to five years are instead giving those tasks to AI tools operated by mid-career professionals. The economics make sense on a quarterly earnings call. One senior developer with Copilot produces what used to take a team of three.

But this creates a pipeline problem that compounds over time. If you stop hiring junior developers in 2024, where do your senior developers come from in 2030? If you eliminate associate analyst roles, who fills the VP of Strategy seat in 2032?

The skills gap data reinforces this. Between 2015 and 2022, about 24% of the skills required for an average job changed. That number is projected to hit 70% by 2030. We're accelerating into a future that demands more experienced, more adaptable workers while simultaneously cutting off the path that creates them.

This is the tension the aggregate job numbers hide. AI isn't killing jobs. It's killing the career ladder. And without the ladder, the jobs at the top eventually have nobody qualified to fill them.

---

## The Founder Explosion

Here's where the story gets interesting. LinkedIn has tracked a 69% increase in people adding "founder" to their profiles since the AI boom began.

Some of that is rebranding unemployment. Let's be honest about it. But a significant portion represents something genuinely new: AI has dropped the cost of starting a business to near zero in ways that weren't possible three years ago.

A solo founder with the right AI stack can now handle customer research, product development, marketing copy, financial modeling, and basic legal review without hiring a single person. That used to require a seed round and five employees. Now it requires a laptop and subscriptions.

The young workers who can't get hired at Google are building their own companies instead. Whether that's a healthy economic shift or a generation making the best of a bad situation probably depends on which founder you ask. But it's happening at scale, and it represents a genuine structural change in how careers begin.

---

## The CEO View vs. The Employee View

From the executive suite, the data looks promising. Productivity is up. Wages for retained employees are rising. AI tools are delivering measurable ROI. The reshaping narrative from BCG feels optimistic and manageable.

From the cubicle, the view is different. You watch three junior colleagues get "restructured" while your own workload doubles. Your raise looks good as a percentage, but it came with the expectation that you now manage AI tools on top of your existing responsibilities. The 16.7% wage increase in your sector doesn't feel like a bonus. It feels like combat pay.

Both perspectives contain truth. The aggregate data is real. The lived experience is also real. The mistake is pretending one invalidates the other.

---

## What Smart Companies Are Doing Differently

The organizations that will come out ahead aren't the ones automating the most aggressively or the ones resisting AI entirely. They're the ones redesigning the ladder itself.

That means creating new entry points. Instead of eliminating junior roles, restructuring them around AI fluency. A junior analyst in 2026 shouldn't be doing the same tasks as a junior analyst in 2020. They should be learning to direct AI systems, validate AI outputs, and handle the judgment calls that AI can't make. The role changes. The role doesn't disappear.

It means investing in internal mobility. If 70% of required skills are going to change by 2030, the companies that build continuous learning into their operating model have a structural advantage over those treating training as an annual checkbox.

And it means being honest about the transition cost. The next five years are going to be uncomfortable for a lot of workers, especially those early in their careers. Pretending the data says otherwise doesn't help them. Acknowledging the gap and building bridges across it does.

---

## The Real Conversation

AI created 1.3 million jobs. That's true. AI is also dismantling the pathways that prepare people for those jobs. That's also true.

The question isn't whether AI creates or destroys jobs. It's whether we're building the systems, the training programs, the career architectures, that let people actually reach the jobs AI is creating.

Right now, we're not. And that's why everyone still feels terrified, no matter what the numbers say.
