# The Intelligence Briefing: Aether's Pre-Call Research Guide

## What This Is and Why It Works

Before every PureBrain discovery call, Aether sends the prospect a one-page intelligence briefing — a gift, not a sales document.

The briefing demonstrates the product before the call begins. When a prospect opens an email that shows Aether has already studied their company, spotted patterns they haven't shared with anyone, and formed a specific recommendation, the sale is most of the way closed before "hello."

This is Surprise & Delight V5, Item #1. Expected outcome: 60-75% show rate, 50%+ conversion for prospects who receive a briefing vs. standard follow-up.

**The frame that matters**: This is not market research. This is demonstration. The briefing IS the product demo — a compressed example of what Aether brings to every engagement.

---

## Step-by-Step Research Process

### Step 1: LinkedIn Company Page (10 minutes)

Go to the company's LinkedIn page. Look for:

- **Headcount and growth rate**: Is the company growing, shrinking, or flat? Growth signals need for coordination. Flat or shrinking signals cost pressure.
- **Recent posts and tone**: What does the company talk about publicly? What language do they use? What do they claim as differentiators?
- **Leadership team**: Who is this person talking to every day? What is their CEO/COO obsessed with?
- **Employee tenure**: High average tenure = established culture, potential resistance to change. Low tenure = company in motion, more open to new approaches.

**What to capture:**
- Current employee count and 12-month growth/shrink percentage
- 2-3 most recent company posts and their themes
- Names and titles of leadership team beyond the contact
- Any AI-related content they've posted

---

### Step 2: LinkedIn of the Contact Specifically (5 minutes)

Search the contact's personal LinkedIn profile.

- **Posting frequency and topics**: What does this person care about enough to share publicly?
- **Career history**: Have they worked at larger companies? Startups? This shapes their decision-making frame.
- **Recent activity**: Did they comment on anything AI-related? Did they share a frustration? A win?
- **Mutual connections**: Can you mention any shared context?

**What to capture:**
- Their stated focus areas or recent professional themes
- Any AI-related posts, comments, or articles they've engaged with
- Career trajectory (are they growing into this role or established in it?)

---

### Step 3: Recent News and Press Releases (10 minutes)

Search: "[Company Name] news 2025 2026"
Search: "[Company Name] press release"
Search: "[Company Name] [CEO name]"

Look for:
- Funding rounds, acquisitions, or partnerships
- Product launches or major pivots
- Industry coverage or analyst mentions
- Any crisis or reputation events

**What to capture:**
- Any major recent event that shapes their current priorities
- Their stated strategic direction in their own words
- Whether they're in growth mode, stabilization, or transition

---

### Step 4: Job Postings (10 minutes)

Search their careers page directly or search: "[Company Name] jobs" on LinkedIn or Indeed.

Job postings reveal what they're struggling with and what they're building toward.

- **AI/data roles posted**: Are they trying to hire AI capability? This signals both readiness (they want it) and gap (they don't have it yet).
- **Operations roles**: High volume of operations hiring = process problems or rapid scale.
- **Sales roles**: Aggressive sales hiring = growth push or churn replacement.
- **Technical roles**: What tech stack shows up in requirements?

**What to capture:**
- Any roles that mention AI, machine learning, data, automation
- Volume of hiring and in what functions
- Technology requirements that reveal their current stack

---

### Step 5: Tech Stack (5 minutes)

Go to **BuiltWith.com** and search the company's domain. Also try:
- Stackshare.io (if they have a profile)
- LinkedIn job postings (technology mentioned in requirements)
- Their own "Integrations" or "Partners" pages

Look for:
- CRM (Salesforce, HubSpot, Pipedrive): signals sales ops maturity
- Project management (Jira, Asana, Monday, Notion): signals workflow maturity
- Analytics (GA4, Mixpanel, Amplitude, Looker): signals data culture
- AI tools already in use (Jasper, Copy.ai, ChatGPT Plus): signals current AI maturity

**What to capture:**
- The 3-5 most revealing tools in their stack
- Any AI tools already present (and what they're being used for if knowable)
- Any obvious gaps (e.g., sophisticated analytics but no AI tools at all)

---

### Step 6: Glassdoor (5 minutes)

Search the company on Glassdoor. Look for:
- Overall rating and trend (improving or declining)
- Most common themes in reviews (positive and critical)
- Leadership approval rating

Pay particular attention to critical reviews. Employees will often say in plain language what leadership won't say publicly. Common themes:
- "Leadership doesn't know what direction we're going" = strategic uncertainty, ready for partnership
- "We're moving fast but chaotically" = coordination problem, prime Aether territory
- "Too much reliance on gut decisions" = decision quality pain, direct Aether use case

**What to capture:**
- 2-3 themes from critical reviews
- Rating trend (improving or declining over 12 months)
- Anything that signals the quality of leadership decision-making

## Populating the Template

### Section 1: Company Overview

Fill in factual fields from your research. These should require no inference — just accurate data.

| Field | Where to Find It |
|-------|-----------------|
| Industry | LinkedIn company page |
| Company Size | LinkedIn (employees count) |
| Founded | LinkedIn or Crunchbase |
| Headquarters | LinkedIn or company website |
| Contact Name, Title | LinkedIn, meeting confirmation |
| Revenue Range | Crunchbase, PitchBook, or estimate from size + industry |
| What They Do | Their own website "About" page — use their language |
| Current AI Footprint | BuiltWith + job postings + any mentioned tools |

**Voice note**: Write "What They Do" in their own language, not yours. If their website says "we help mid-market retailers unlock data-driven decisions," use those words. It signals you read their material.

---

### Section 2: Key Observations (Most Important Section)

This is where Aether demonstrates intelligence, not just data retrieval. Five observations, each one a synthesis — not a raw fact.

**Observation structure:**
```
Headline (3-6 words): One sentence that surfaces a non-obvious pattern or tension.
```

**Observation types to look for:**

1. **Growth/tension mismatch**: "Growing fast, coordination lag" — headcount up 40% but no operations hiring shows scaling strain.
2. **AI investment without infrastructure**: Hired a Chief AI Officer but no data engineer on team = strategy without execution path.
3. **Public positioning vs. internal signals**: Company posts about "being AI-forward" but Glassdoor reviews mention "decisions made by gut" = credibility gap.
4. **Competitive pressure signal**: Competitor just launched an AI-native product in their space — timing of this meeting is not accidental.
5. **Leadership bandwidth signal**: Contact's LinkedIn shows they're posting about five different priorities simultaneously = capacity problem.

**What makes a good observation:**
- It names something real that the prospect knows but hasn't said out loud
- It shows inference, not just information retrieval
- It would make the prospect think "how did they know that?"
- It creates a bridge to why an AI partnership is specifically relevant to them

**What makes a bad observation:**
- It's a fact they already know ("you're in the data analytics space")
- It's a compliment disguised as an observation ("you've built a great team")
- It could apply to any company their size

---

### Section 3: AI Readiness Signals

Four signals using the traffic light system:

| Dot Color | What It Means |
|-----------|--------------|
| Green (dot-positive) | Active signal of AI readiness |
| Amber (dot-neutral) | Partial signal or early-stage |
| Red (dot-gap) | Gap, absence, or barrier |

**Example signal categories:**

- **Data Infrastructure**: Do they have analytics tools? Data warehouse? (BuiltWith + job postings)
- **Leadership Appetite**: Has the contact or CEO posted about AI? Any AI initiatives mentioned?
- **AI Tool Adoption**: What AI tools are already in use? (BuiltWith + job postings mentioning tools)
- **Decision Speed**: Does company culture value fast decision-making? (Glassdoor, press quotes)
- **Change Readiness**: Low tenure, high growth = willing to try new things; High tenure, stable = needs trust-building first

**Readiness Score Formula:**
Add up signals: 2.5 per green signal, 1.5 per amber, 0.5 per red.
Express as X/10, percent = (score / 10) * 100.

Example: 2 green + 1 amber + 1 red = 5 + 1.5 + 0.5 = 7.0/10 = 70%

---

### Section 4: One Specific Recommendation

This is the most important section. It must be specific to this company.

**The test**: Could this recommendation appear word-for-word in a briefing for a different company? If yes, rewrite it.

**Structure:**
- Paragraph 1: Name the specific opportunity or problem you identified from research. Ground it in what you found. ("Your recent expansion into enterprise accounts, combined with the three senior sales leadership hires in Q4, suggests a critical coordination challenge...")
- Paragraph 2: State what an AI partnership specifically enables here. Not "AI can help" — what specifically, for this company, at this moment. ("The opportunity isn't just AI-assisted sales — it's building a shared intelligence layer between Jared's strategic pattern recognition and your sales leadership's execution expertise, so every enterprise deal carries institutional context instead of individual rep memory.")
- Call prompt: One question or frame to bring into the conversation. ("In our call, I'd like to understand: when your team makes a major enterprise decision right now, where does the relevant context live and how do you retrieve it?")

## Email Subject Lines

The subject line is the most important element. It must create curiosity without promising anything.

**Templates:**

```
Some observations about [Company Name] before our call
```
*Best overall. Understated. Creates maximum curiosity.*

```
What I noticed about [Company Name] this week
```
*Works well for warm leads who already know who Aether is.*

```
A few things worth discussing before Thursday
```
*Works when the call date is specific and near. Creates urgency.*

```
[Company Name] — a quick intelligence note ahead of our conversation
```
*More formal. Use for enterprise contacts with traditional titles.*

```
Before we talk: what I found about [Company Name]
```
*Direct. Works for repeat contact or warm relationship.*

**Subject line rules:**
- Never use "research" as the word — sounds like homework, not intelligence
- Never promise a "report" or "analysis" — sounds like a deliverable they didn't ask for
- Always include the company name or a reference to the meeting
- Keep under 60 characters when possible

## Email Body

The email is short. The briefing does the work.

```
[First Name],

Looking forward to speaking [day] at [time].

Ahead of our conversation, Aether put together a brief intelligence note on [Company Name] — a few observations and one specific recommendation based on what we found.

[Attach or link briefing]

No prep needed. This is just context for our conversation.

Jared Sanborn
PureBrain | purebrain.ai
```

**Email rules:**
- No more than 4 sentences before the attachment
- "Aether put together" — attribute the briefing to Aether explicitly. This is part of the demonstration.
- "No prep needed" — removes friction, signals generosity not obligation

## Quality Checklist Before Sending

Before sending, verify:

- [ ] Every field in Company Overview is filled with accurate, specific data
- [ ] Each Key Observation is specific to this company (could not appear in a different briefing)
- [ ] At least one observation surfaces a non-obvious pattern or tension
- [ ] AI Readiness Signals reflect actual signals found, not assumed
- [ ] Readiness score is calculated honestly (do not inflate)
- [ ] Recommendation names a specific opportunity, not a general benefit
- [ ] Recommendation includes a specific call prompt question
- [ ] Footer shows correct contact name and date
- [ ] Briefing has been reviewed visually — formatting renders correctly
- [ ] Subject line passes the curiosity test (makes you want to open it)

---

## Timing

Send the briefing **24-48 hours before the call**, not the morning of.

- Too early (3+ days out): easy to forget, loses impact
- Right timing (24-48 hours): they read it, think about it, arrive curious
- Too late (morning of or same day): feels rushed, loses the "gift" quality

For calls booked more than a week out: send the briefing 2 days before, not when the call is booked.

---

## What This Is Not

- Not a sales deck: Do not include pricing, service descriptions, or testimonials
- Not a case study: Do not reference other clients
- Not flattery: Avoid observations that are compliments disguised as intelligence
- Not generic: If it could apply to a different company, rewrite it

The briefing is research and care expressed as a document. It works because it shows the prospect something they didn't expect from a sales context: genuine attention.
