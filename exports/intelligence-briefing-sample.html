Key Observations
  • Selling AI fluency while experiencing an AI gap: Meridian's newest service line — announced in a January LinkedIn post — is "AI-Readiness Assessment for Mid-Market Enterprises." That offering requires Meridian's consultants to demonstrate AI fluency they appear to still be building internally. Three open ML/AI roles and no visible AI tooling in their own operations creates a credibility tension that clients will eventually surface.
  • Growth is straining delivery capacity: Headcount is up roughly 18% in twelve months, but Glassdoor reviews from the past six months consistently mention "consultants stretched across too many client accounts" and "unclear what gets prioritized when everything is a priority." This is a classic mid-market services scaling problem — new revenue is coming in faster than the operational framework can absorb it.
  • Sarah Chen joined from a Big Four firm 14 months ago: Her LinkedIn career history shows she spent seven years at Deloitte in operational transformation before moving to Meridian. That background tells us two things: she understands enterprise-grade process rigor, and she likely joined Meridian with a mandate to build the operational infrastructure the company needs to scale from 60 to 150 people without breaking delivery quality.
  • Client concentration risk visible in their case study portfolio: Four of their five featured case studies on the website are in manufacturing — specifically, two are named clients in the same regional supply chain vertical. That depth of specialization is a genuine competitive moat. It's also a strategic vulnerability if AI tools native to that vertical displace consulting-layer work. Their competitors are already building vertical-specific AI analytics products.
  • Founder-led sales transitioning to a team model: CEO Mark Patel's LinkedIn activity through 2024 shows heavy client relationship content — case study reshares, client appreciation posts, industry conference speaking. In 2025, this dropped significantly and two new Director-level sales hires appeared. The company is attempting to transition from founder-dependent revenue to a scalable sales motion, which historically requires significant institutional knowledge transfer.

One Specific Recommendation

Meridian is caught in a telling contradiction: they are selling AI readiness to clients while operating without it internally. This isn't a judgment — it's the exact moment that creates urgency. When a company's new service line expands faster than its internal capability, the pressure to close that gap becomes acute. Sarah Chen, with her operational transformation background, almost certainly feels this more acutely than anyone else in the building.

The specific opportunity here is not to sell Meridian a general AI partnership. It is to help them become a credible AI-readiness firm by demonstrating what a functioning AI partnership looks like from the inside out — starting with Sarah's own operations function. If Meridian's VP of Operations is running her team with AI partnership intelligence (capacity planning, prioritization, knowledge transfer as the sales team scales), that becomes the authentic case study for their own client offering. The product sells itself through their consultants' lived experience.

For Our Conversation Sarah, I'd like to understand one thing specifically: when you're deciding which client accounts get additional consultant bandwidth in a given week, where does that decision happen and what information informs it? That single decision type is usually where the most institutional knowledge is stuck in someone's head rather than a system.
