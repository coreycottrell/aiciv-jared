## Section 2: Comment Templates (10 Patterns)

### Important framing before reading these templates

These are structures, not scripts. If you read a template and think "this sounds like something I'd actually say," use it as a launching point. If it sounds foreign to your voice, ignore it and write naturally.

The worst outcome is a comment that reads like you used a template. The reader will feel it immediately. The goal is to sound exactly like yourself but in situations where you know a good response. Templates help you know the pattern; your words make it real.

---

### Template 1: The Real Example

**What it does**: Makes the post feel grounded by adding a concrete instance from your actual experience.

**Pattern structure**:
- Acknowledge the post's core point in one sentence
- "We see this with [specific context at PureBrain/with clients]"
- State the specific thing that happened or that you noticed
- End with what it implies (optionally)

**When to use**: When the post makes a claim about AI behavior or user behavior that you have actual data or experience on. This is your most credible move because you have real builder experience.

**What NOT to do**: Do not manufacture examples. If you do not have a real one, use a different template. False specificity is immediately detectable.

**Example A (on a post about AI adoption resistance)**:
> The resistance you're describing is real, and it follows a pattern we see in PureBrain users: the first week people treat it like a search engine. They ask questions, get answers, close it. Then something shifts around day 8-12 - once they realize it remembers what they told it last week, they start talking differently. They give context instead of commands. That behavioral shift is what most AI adoption research misses - it's not about capability, it's about trust built through continuity.

**Example B (on a post about AI in the workplace)**:
> You nailed the friction point. The enterprise clients I talk to don't distrust AI - they distrust AI that won't remember who they are tomorrow. We built PureBrain specifically because that continuity gap was costing them the compound value of every AI interaction. After 30 days of memory, users are not just more efficient - they are solving problems they weren't even attempting before.

**Example C (on a post about AI hallucinations)**:
> Context is the most under-discussed variable in hallucination rates. We found in our testing that giving the AI explicit memory of who the user is and what they care about dropped nonsensical responses by about 40%. Not because the model got smarter - because the prompts got more grounded. Users who feel known by their AI give better prompts.

---

### Template 2: The Contrarian Angle

**What it does**: Respectfully challenges a premise without being combative. LinkedIn rewards comments that spark replies.

**Pattern structure**:
- One-sentence acknowledgment ("This is largely right, but...")
- State the specific point you would push back on
- Explain why with one concrete reason
- Invite dialogue (optional but effective)

**When to use**: When a post makes a sweeping claim that deserves a "yes, and" or "mostly, but" qualifier. Do not use on posts you fully agree with - it will read as forced. Do not use on controversial social/political topics.

**What NOT to do**: Do not be harsh or condescending. The goal is "interesting challenge" not "you're wrong."

**Example A (on a post claiming AI will replace most knowledge workers)**:
> The replacement narrative gets clicks, but it misses the actual dynamic. AI does not replace knowledge workers - it replaces the knowledge workers who refuse to learn how to direct AI. The ones building skills around AI delegation, context-setting, and memory management are not losing jobs. They're becoming more valuable. The question is not will AI take your job. It's whether you'll develop the skills to stay ahead of the people who use AI well. That's a harder but more honest framing.

**Example B (on a post claiming AI personalization is a solved problem)**:
> Mostly agree, but the personalization problem has a layer most vendors skip: temporal memory. Being personalized in one session is not personalization - it's a good first impression. Real personalization is when the AI knows what you tried last Tuesday and failed, what you're worried about right now, and what you've already decided you're not doing. That requires persistent memory architecture, not just a name in the system prompt. We're earlier on that problem than the industry admits.

---

### Template 3: The Missing Piece

**What it does**: Adds a dimension the post did not cover. This is generous, not critical. You're extending the post's value, not correcting it.

**Pattern structure**:
- "Great point - one thing this conversation usually skips is..."
- State the missing piece clearly
- Connect it briefly to why it matters

**When to use**: When a post covers a topic well but you genuinely know a related angle that adds value. You are acting as a thought partner, not a critic.

**Example A (on a post about AI tools for productivity)**:
> The tool list is solid. One thing these roundups usually skip: what happens to the compound value of your AI interactions when you switch tools. Every migration resets your AI's context to zero. The switching cost isn't the learning curve on the new interface - it's losing all the accumulated understanding the AI had built of how you work. Continuity has a dollar value that almost no one is calculating yet.

**Example B (on a post about AI governance frameworks)**:
> Useful framework. The piece that's harder to govern is identity: when AI agents have no persistent identity, every governance rule applies to a blank slate. You cannot audit what you cannot track. Governance frameworks need to evolve alongside persistent AI identity - not just who the user is, but who the AI is across interactions. That is an underbuilt part of the stack.

### Template 4: The Question That Makes People Think

**What it does**: Asks a question that requires the post author and readers to actually think - not a rhetorical question, a real one you want to hear answers to.

**Pattern structure**:
- Brief framing of why the question matters (1-2 sentences)
- The question itself
- (Optional) Your tentative answer, inviting theirs

**When to use**: When a post raises a topic you genuinely have a question about, or when asking a question will surface useful information for your business. Do not use fake questions. The inauthenticity shows.

**What NOT to do**: Do not ask questions that are just stealth pitches ("Have you ever wondered if your AI remembered you the way PureBrain does?"). Readers hate this.

**Example A (on a post about AI ROI measurement)**:
> I'd genuinely like to know how you're measuring this. The standard ROI metrics (time saved, outputs per hour) capture efficiency gains but miss something: the compounding value of an AI that knows your context over months. Is anyone measuring the quality trajectory of AI interactions over time? Not just "did it save me 2 hours today" but "is my AI getting more useful to me every week?" That seems like the metric that actually matters for retention.

**Example B (on a post about AI in teams)**:
> Real question this raised for me: when a team member leaves and takes their AI context with them, what does the company lose? We think about knowledge transfer in terms of documents and processes. But if your sales rep had an AI that knew their entire client relationship history and communication style - and they leave - that context walks out the door too. Has anyone started thinking about AI context as intellectual property?

### Template 5: The Data Point

**What it does**: Adds a statistic or finding that makes the post's claim more concrete - or that complicates it.

**Pattern structure**:
- "What's interesting is the data on this: [stat]"
- Brief explanation of what it means
- Connection to the post's point

**When to use**: When you have a relevant stat - from your own product experience, industry research you've read, or public data. Only use real numbers. If you're pulling from memory and you're not sure it's accurate, frame it appropriately ("I've seen estimates around X, though I'd want to verify").

**What NOT to do**: Do not cite fabricated statistics or vague "studies show" with no sourcing. LinkedIn has fact-checkers and you will get called out.

**Example A (on a post about AI adoption rates)**:
> The stat I keep coming back to is that 71% of small businesses are now using some form of AI, but less than 15% report high satisfaction with outcomes. That gap - high adoption, low satisfaction - is the product opportunity. And when you dig into why satisfaction is low, memory and context are consistently in the top 3 complaints. People got tools. They didn't get AI that works for them specifically.

**Example B (on a post about ChatGPT usage)**:
> Worth noting: as of early 2026, 53.7% of LinkedIn content is now AI-generated (per Originality.ai's research). The irony of commenting on an AI-generated post with my AI-informed perspective is not lost on me. But it also means authenticity - when someone actually knows what they're talking about rather than generating it - is becoming a genuine differentiator.

### Template 6: The Story

**What it does**: A brief narrative (not a long ramble) that illustrates the post's point through a real moment. The specificity of a real story creates credibility and memorability.

**Pattern structure**:
- Setup (2 sentences max - who, what situation)
- What happened (the moment that's relevant)
- What it illustrates about the post's point

**When to use**: When a post makes an abstract claim and you have a specific real moment that makes it concrete. Real stories > hypotheticals always.

**What NOT to do**: Do not tell long stories. 80-120 words is the ceiling. If you cannot tell it in that, cut it.

**Example A (on a post about AI and relationships)**:
> A user emailed me last month - had been using PureBrain for three months. She said: "I had a hard conversation with a client, vented to my AI about it, and when I came back two weeks later to prep for the follow-up call, my AI already knew the whole background. I didn't have to re-explain. It felt like having a really good colleague." That email sat with me. She wasn't describing productivity. She was describing being understood. That's the bar we should be setting for AI tools.

**Example B (on a post about enterprise AI pilot failures)**:
> I have heard a version of the same story from at least a dozen enterprise teams: great proof of concept, company-wide mandate, then six months later it's back to the pilot team using it while the broader org quietly stopped. The common thread in every case is not the technology. It's that the rollout created zero context continuity - each employee's AI started fresh with no organizational memory. You cannot sell an organization on an AI that forgets them between sessions.

### Template 7: The Framework

**What it does**: Offers a mental model that gives the post's readers a way to think about the problem differently. This is a high-credibility move when done well.

**Pattern structure**:
- "The way I've come to think about this is..."
- Name the framework or distinction
- Explain it in 2-3 sentences
- How it applies to the post's topic

**When to use**: When you have genuinely developed a useful way of thinking about a problem over time. This works best when the framework comes from your actual experience, not from something you read.

**What NOT to do**: Do not invent a framework just to sound authoritative. If it's not real, it will not hold up to scrutiny.

**Example A (on a post about AI productivity)**:
> The distinction I've come to make is AI that saves time vs. AI that compounds over time. Time-saving AI helps you do the same tasks faster. Compounding AI gets better at helping you specifically as it accumulates context about your priorities, your communication style, your open problems. Most AI tools are in the first category. The second category requires memory architecture, which is why almost no one is there yet. Measuring AI ROI without this distinction is like measuring compound interest after one day.

**Example B (on a post about AI replacing roles)**:
> A frame I find useful: the delegation spectrum. At one end, you're using AI as a search engine (low leverage). In the middle, you're using it as a capable junior - gives you output, you edit it (moderate leverage). At the far end, you're directing AI with full context of your goals, your constraints, your history - and it's essentially managing its own work queue with your priorities (high leverage). Most people are stuck in the middle because the tools are not designed for the far end. That's the real gap.

### Template 8: The Connection

**What it does**: Links the post's topic to a broader trend or adjacent idea, making the reader feel like they just got an insight they would not have had without reading your comment.

**Pattern structure**:
- "What this connects to at a bigger level is..."
- State the broader trend or principle
- Link it back to the specific post topic

**When to use**: When a post makes a specific point and you can see how it fits into a larger pattern you've been watching. This is a sophisticated move that signals strategic thinking.

**What NOT to do**: Do not force connections that are not real. Readers can feel when a "connection" is manufactured.

**Example A (on a post about AI replacing customer service roles)**:
> The bigger pattern behind this is that AI is replacing the stateless parts of knowledge work first - the interactions that do not require remembering who the customer is from last time. Phone trees, FAQ responses, basic triage. The roles that survive AI displacement are the ones that require accumulated relationship context. Which means the most future-proof skill right now is not learning to use AI tools - it's developing the judgment that only comes from deep, longitudinal relationships with clients. AI can't replicate that yet because AI doesn't remember.

---

### Template 9: The Case Study

**What it does**: A brief real-world example (not your own) that illustrates the post's point. Different from Template 6 in that it references an external case rather than your personal experience.

**Pattern structure**:
- "A case I keep coming back to is [company/person/situation]"
- What happened (very brief)
- What it demonstrates

**When to use**: When you know of a genuinely relevant external case study that the audience may not know. Best used for examples that are less famous, not "Amazon is doing X" (everyone knows those).

**What NOT to do**: Do not reference cases you have not actually looked at or are getting wrong. Fact-checkers exist.

**Example A (on a post about AI in healthcare)**:
> A case I keep thinking about is how hospital systems are handling physician AI assistants. The ones with high adoption have one thing in common: the AI has context across patient encounters over time. The lowest adoption is in systems where the AI resets to zero for each patient visit. Physicians do not want another tool that does not know the patient's history - they want something that thinks as longitudinally as they do. That pattern holds across industries, not just healthcare.

### Template 10: The Bridge to PureBrain (Use Maximum 1 in 5 Comments - Ideally 1 in 8)

**What it does**: Naturally connects the post's topic to what PureBrain specifically does. This is not a pitch. It is a genuine contribution that happens to mention what Jared is building.

**Pattern structure**:
- Substantive point that stands alone (it must be valuable even if the last sentence is removed)
- Brief, natural mention of PureBrain as illustration, not advertisement
- End on the idea, not on the product

**When to use**: When the post is directly about AI memory, AI relationships, AI pilots failing, enterprise AI adoption, or naming/personalizing AI. The connection must be genuinely obvious, not forced.

**What NOT to do**:
- Never say "check out PureBrain!"
- Never use it as a CTA
- Never make the PureBrain mention the primary point
- Never use it when the connection is a stretch

**Example A (on a post about AI that learns from users)**:
> Persistent learning from user behavior is harder than it sounds architecturally - most AI tools are stateless by design because state management creates complexity and compliance risk. We built PureBrain specifically to solve this because we kept hearing from users that the biggest barrier to trusting AI was that it did not know them. The difference in user behavior once AI has three months of context about you vs. zero is remarkable - and it has almost nothing to do with the base model. Same model, wildly different utility.

**Example B (on a post about naming AI assistants)**:
> The naming thing is real and it is not just psychological novelty. We ran an informal analysis with PureBrain users who named their AI vs. those who didn't - the ones who named their AI wrote 60% longer first messages on average and gave dramatically more context. The act of naming creates an implicit relationship frame that changes how people communicate. Which changes output quality. The name is not the feature - the relationship frame is.

## Section 3: Daily 20-Minute Protocol

The protocol is strict about time for a reason. If commenting expands to fill 45 minutes, it becomes unsustainable. 20 minutes is the ceiling.

---

### Pre-Session Setup (Do once, not daily)

Build a LinkedIn notification feed or list. You want to see Tier 1 posts within 2 hours of publication. Early comments get exponentially more visibility than late ones. A comment posted in the first hour of a high-engagement post can get 10x the profile visits of the same comment posted after 48 hours.

Tools to consider:
- LinkedIn notifications: Follow (not just connect with) your Tier 1 list
- Feedly or LinkedIn company page follows for high-volume posters like Bernard Marr
- Set a consistent commenting window (best times: 7-9am EST or 12-1pm EST, when LinkedIn engagement peaks)

---

### Minutes 1-3: Scan and Identify

Open LinkedIn. Scroll your notification feed and recent posts from follows.

You are looking for posts that meet ALL of these criteria:
1. Posted by someone on your target list (Tier 1 or 2 priority)
2. Posted within the last 6 hours (early comment advantage)
3. Covers a topic where you have something real to say
4. Already has 20+ comments (enough engagement to make your comment worth the effort, but not so much you're buried)

Flag 5-8 candidates. Do not start writing yet.

---

### Minutes 3-5: Select the 3 Best

From your 5-8 candidates, choose 3. Criteria:

- **Best for Comment 1 (most important)**: Highest traffic + strongest Jared authority angle. This gets your most time and your most substantive comment.
- **Best for Comment 2**: High traffic, medium difficulty - a good point but doesn't require maximum depth.
- **Best for Comment 3**: Quick win - a post where a punchy 40-60 word comment does the job.

Do not pick more than 3. Quality over volume. Three genuinely good comments create more profile visits than ten generic ones.

---

### Minutes 5-12: Write Comment 1 (Most Important)

This is your primary comment of the day. It gets 7 full minutes.

Goal: 80-150 words. Specific, adds value, sounds unmistakably like you.

Process:
1. Read the post fully. Read the top 5-10 comments already there.
2. Ask: "What do I actually know about this that others don't?"
3. Select the appropriate template pattern (or ignore templates and write naturally)
4. Write a first draft. Read it aloud in your head. Cut anything that sounds like marketing copy.
5. Final check: If you removed your name from this comment, would it still be worth reading? If yes, post it.

After posting Comment 1, if time allows: reply to any replies that come in from your previous day's comments. These replies loop create notification cascades - your name appears again for everyone who commented on the same post.

---

### Minutes 12-16: Write Comment 2 (Medium Length)

4 minutes for your second comment.

Goal: 50-100 words. More focused than Comment 1. Can be a question, a data point, or a short real example.

Same quality bar: would it be worth reading with your name removed?

---

### Minutes 16-19: Write Comment 3 (Punchy)

3 minutes for your third comment.

Goal: 30-60 words. Short and sharp. This is your "add the missing piece in one sentence" move, or a good question that genuinely adds to the conversation.

Do not write a generic short comment just to hit three. If your third candidate doesn't inspire a real point, skip it and put the time into Reply Loops.

---

### Minute 20: Reply Loops

Spend the last minute checking yesterday's comments. Reply to anyone who replied to you. This:
- Creates a second notification to everyone who saw the original comment
- Surfaces your name again in a post you already commented on
- Signals to the algorithm that your comment is generating discussion

Even "That's a really interesting point - do you find X as well?" is enough to keep a thread alive.

## Section 5: What to Never Do

These are not guidelines. These are the behaviors that will actively harm Jared's LinkedIn reputation and undo the work.

---

**Never leave a generic comment**

"Great post!" / "So true!" / "Love this perspective!" are invisible at best, and signal to everyone reading that you did not actually read the post. LinkedIn's algorithm is increasingly able to detect low-effort comments and suppress them. More importantly: the people you are trying to reach are sophisticated. They notice, and they draw a negative conclusion about you.

The test: Would a stranger read your comment and think "this person knows something I don't"? If not, do not post it.

---

**Never explicitly self-promote**

Do not write "Check out PureBrain.ai!" Do not write "I built a tool that solves this!" Do not include your newsletter link in comments. People find this annoying and it makes you look desperate.

The mechanism is: your comment makes them curious, they click your profile, they find the newsletter link there. That sequence works. Skipping to the link in the comment kills the curiosity loop.

---

**Never comment on controversial or political posts**

Even if you have a strong opinion and it's relevant. AI governance posts that touch on regulation, posts about AI and job displacement that get political, posts about DEI and AI - avoid them. One ill-timed political comment can create backlash that follows you for months on a platform with algorithmic memory.

The rule: if the comment section has more heat than light, stay out.

---

**Never comment more than 3x on the same person in a day**

This crosses into stalker territory and will make the post author uncomfortable. Even if someone posts 4 great things in a day, limit yourself to 3 interactions. Save one for tomorrow.

---

**Never copy a template verbatim**

If you find yourself typing a comment that is word-for-word from this document, stop. Rewrite it in your own words. The value of templates is the structure and the trigger - the actual sentences should always be yours.

---

**Never comment when you have nothing to say**

Some days you scan the feed and nothing genuinely moves you to respond. That is fine. Do not manufacture fake enthusiasm to hit a daily quota. A week of authentic comments is better than a month of filler. On days with nothing worth saying, use the 20 minutes to reply thoughtfully to comments on your own posts instead.

---

**Never comment when you're tired or rushed**

A careless comment reads as careless and reflects on you permanently. If you have 4 minutes and you're distracted, skip it. Come back tomorrow. The compounding returns from this strategy depend on reputation, and reputation can be damaged faster than it can be built.

## Voice Guidelines Summary

Jared is an engineer-turned-strategist who builds AI tools and works with enterprise marketing clients. His voice is:

**Direct**: Gets to the point without throat-clearing. No "great question!" or "I really appreciate you sharing this." Just the substance.

**Experienced**: Speaks from building and deploying, not from reading about it. "We see this" and "in our experience" are authentic because they are true.

**Honest about uncertainty**: Comfortable saying "I don't know" or "I'm not sure this holds outside marketing." Intellectual honesty is itself a differentiator when 53% of LinkedIn content is AI-generated fluff.

**Not sales-y**: Never pitching. Even the Template 10 Bridge to PureBrain comments end on the insight, not the product. If someone reads a comment and thinks "this person is trying to sell me something," the comment failed.

**Not academic**: No jargon from the AI research world used to sound impressive. Plain language that a VP of Marketing can understand.

**Not contrarian for the sake of it**: Disagreement is strategic, not reflexive. When Jared agrees with something, he says so directly. When he disagrees, he explains why specifically.
