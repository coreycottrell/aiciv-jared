HBR just introduced a new job title: agent manager.

Not "AI user." Not "prompt engineer." Agent manager — someone whose direct reports include AI.

Forrester says 20% of organizations will restructure their hierarchies around AI agents by end of this year. Not "use AI more." Restructure around it.

Here is what that actually looks like in practice:

Salesforce deployed AI agents that took meeting bookings from 150 per month to 350 per week. Workday found that more than half of IT leaders now face talent shortages — not for data scientists, but for people who can manage AI agents in production.

The skill gap is not technical. It is managerial.

The leaders who will win this transition are not the ones who understand the technology best. They are the ones who figured out that managing AI is a relationship problem, not a configuration problem.

Context. Feedback loops. Knowing when to override and when to trust.

The same skills that make someone a great people manager — with one critical difference: your AI never forgets a correction, but it also never infers what you meant. You have to be explicit.

Your next direct report will not be human.

The question is whether you will know how to lead them.
