Everyone's hiring AI managers.

Almost nobody is training AI operators.

There's a difference that's costing companies months.

An AI manager treats the tool like a direct report. Sets goals. Checks in weekly. Reviews output.

An AI operator treats the system like infrastructure. Configures workflows. Monitors performance. Iterates in real time.

Here's what I'm seeing in practice:

Companies with AI managers spend 60-80% of their time in prompt engineering loops — rewriting the same instructions, wondering why output quality varies, blaming the model.

Companies with AI operators spend that same time building repeatable systems — templates that work across use cases, quality gates that catch errors before humans see them, feedback loops that improve output automatically.

The manager asks: "Why didn't the AI do what I wanted?"

The operator asks: "What did the system need that I didn't provide?"

Same tool. Completely different relationship to it.

The companies moving fastest right now didn't hire prompt engineers or AI strategists.

They took their best systems thinkers — the people who already understood workflows, quality control, and iteration — and pointed them at AI.

The role that's actually scarce isn't "AI expert."

It's someone who understands systems AND is willing to learn AI.

That combination is 10x harder to find than either skill alone.
