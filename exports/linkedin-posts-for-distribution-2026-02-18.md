### Draft Post

Here's a dynamic I see in almost every organization adopting AI right now:

The CEO is excited. The team is anxious.

And neither one tells the other.

Leadership sees AI as a competitive edge - a way to move faster, cut costs, and stay ahead. Employees see AI as something that might make their role smaller. Both perspectives are understandable. Neither is wrong. But when those two views never get put on the same table, you get something worse than disagreement.

You get parallel realities.

The CEO approves a new AI tool. The team quietly doesn't use it. Or uses it just enough to look compliant. Adoption metrics look fine. Actual value: near zero.

I've been thinking about why this happens, and it comes down to something simple: no one has defined what "good AI use" looks like at the individual contributor level.

Leaders are often thinking at the strategy layer - "What can AI do for our business?" But their people are thinking at the task layer - "What does this mean for my specific job?" Those two conversations need to happen in the same room, not in separate silos.

The organizations getting real results from AI are the ones where leadership hasn't just announced a direction - they've built a shared language for what it means to use AI well.

That's harder than buying a tool. And it matters more.

I wrote about this gap in more detail on the blog. Worth a read if you're in the middle of an AI rollout.

### Draft Post

What if every conversation with your most trusted advisor started from scratch?

No memory of what you've already tried. No context on how you think. No understanding of what matters to you. Just a blank slate, every single time.

That's the AI most people are using right now.

Generic AI treats every session as the first session. It doesn't know your industry, your decision-making style, your past mistakes, or your goals. It gives you competent outputs. But competent isn't the same as compounding.

Here's the distinction I find useful: there's a difference between AI that answers your questions and AI that actually understands you over time.

When AI has persistent memory, something different happens. It stops being a tool you query and starts being a system that actually builds on your history. Context accumulates. Patterns become visible. Recommendations get sharper the longer you work together.

The analogy I use is an advisor who's been with you for three years versus one you just hired. Both might be talented. But the one with context wins almost every time.

Most AI conversations are one-and-done. That's not a feature gap - it's a design philosophy gap.

I wrote more about what persistent memory actually enables in practice - not the technical side, but the practical business side.

### Draft Post

I've watched enterprise AI evaluations fall apart at a very specific moment.

Someone in the room asks: "Where does our data go?"

And the silence that follows is the answer.

This isn't a technical problem. It's a trust problem. And it's more common than vendors want to admit.

Most AI agents are built to impress in demos. They're fast, they're capable, they generate outputs that look great in a slideshow. But enterprise buyers have a different checklist than demo audiences do. They need to know: Is this compliant with our data policies? Who has access to our conversations? What happens if we end the contract?

A lot of AI tools don't have clean answers to those questions. Not because they're irresponsible - but because data governance was an afterthought to the feature roadmap.

The result is that organizations end up using AI at the edges - low-stakes tasks, internal experiments - because they can't confidently bring it into high-value workflows. The tool exists, but trust doesn't.

This is the enterprise AI trust gap. And it's probably slowing down more AI adoption than any other single factor.

If you're evaluating AI tools for serious business use, the questions you ask in the security review are more important than anything in the feature tour.

I wrote about what those questions should be on the blog.
