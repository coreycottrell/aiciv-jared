Salesforce deployed AI agents for sales development. Meeting bookings went from 150/month to 350/week. $60 million in annualized pipeline within four months.

Those agents aren't answering FAQs. They have assigned roles. Performance metrics. They sit inside org charts.

And someone has to manage them.

Here's the problem: most leaders are treating AI agents like software. Define the inputs, get the outputs. If it breaks, call IT.

AI agents don't work like that.

An agent's performance is a function of three things: the task it's given, the context it carries, and the feedback loop that shapes its behavior over time.

Get one wrong and you get confident errors at scale.

The leaders who studied this closely — HBR's analysis of the most effective "agent managers" — found something surprising. The best ones didn't come from technical backgrounds. They came from roles requiring service quality judgment and operational experience.

The key trait wasn't technical sophistication. It was what they called "earnest curiosity" — treating an AI agent as something to be understood, not just configured.

By end of 2026, IDC predicts half of AI-enabled enterprise apps will require dedicated oversight positions. Workday found that more than half of IT leaders face AI talent shortages — not for data scientists, but for people who can orchestrate agents in production.

Your next direct report will not be human.

The question is whether you'll know how to manage them.

## First Comment (post separately):

Three things that determine AI agent performance — and none of them are technical:

1. Context depth (does it know your business?)
2. Feedback architecture (are you systematically improving it?)
3. Hybrid workflow design (are humans and AI in the right seats?)

Most organizations have zero process for any of these. Which one is your biggest gap?
