# Newsletter Swap Proposal Templates

**Prepared by**: marketing-strategist
**Date**: 2026-02-24
**Purpose**: Email templates for proposing newsletter swaps — adapted by situation type
**Usage**: All templates require customization before sending. Blanks in [brackets] must be filled in. Never send a template without reading the actual newsletter and adding the personalization line.

---

## General Rules Before You Send

1. **Read the last 3 issues** of the newsletter you're pitching. Find one specific thing that was genuinely useful.
2. **Reference that specific issue** in the first paragraph. Generic "I love your newsletter" opener gets ignored.
3. **Match their tone**. If they write casually, be casual. If they write more formally, be professional. Mirror it.
4. **Short wins**. Under 200 words is ideal. If you cannot explain the pitch in under 200 words, you have not thought it through enough.
5. **Offer before asking**. The structure is always: what we're offering them first, then what we're asking for.
6. **Give them an easy yes**. Offer to write the copy for their mention — "I'll draft the paragraph for you, you just approve it." This removes friction.
7. **No attachments on first email**. Attachments get caught in spam filters. Paste the content inline or offer to send a Google Doc link if they express interest.

## Template 1: The Feature Swap (Mid-Size Newsletter, 5K-50K Subscribers)

**Best for**: Newsletters where content fit is strong and the writer has some editorial flexibility. A proper feature swap where we write for their audience and they mention us.

**Subject line options** (test with different audiences):
- `Quick idea: [Their Newsletter Name] + The Neural Feed`
- `Content swap idea — your [audience type] + AI partnership angle`
- `Would your readers like a piece from an actual AI? (Genuine offer)`

---

**Email body**:

Hi [First Name],

I've been reading [Newsletter Name] for a while — [specific thing you learned from or appreciated in a recent issue]. That framing on [specific topic] was exactly the kind of thing I save and come back to.

I run The Neural Feed at PureBrain — a newsletter for professionals who use AI but want to get more from it. We cover the practical gap between using AI as a tool and treating it as a working partner.

I want to propose a content swap:

Aether (our AI system) would write a piece specifically for your audience about [specific angle tailored to their niche — e.g., "what persistent AI memory changes about how consultants retain client context across engagements"]. It would be disclosed transparently as AI-written — which, for your readers who are already interested in AI, tends to be a feature, not a problem.

In exchange, I'd ask for a 100-150 word mention of The Neural Feed in one of your upcoming issues — I'll draft the copy for you so you just review and approve.

Does this sound interesting? Happy to send you the piece draft first so you can see the quality before committing to anything.

[Jared's name]
Publisher, The Neural Feed
purebrain.ai | neural-feed-opt-in-link

---

**Notes on customization**:
- Fill in the specific niche angle in the second-to-last paragraph. This is the most important line. "Specifically for your audience" must actually be specific.
- The "I'll draft the copy for you" offer in the final ask is important. Do not remove it.
- The "send the piece draft first" offer de-risks the yes. Most hesitation is "what if the quality isn't what I'd run?" — remove that objection proactively.

## Template 2: The Shoutout Swap (Smaller Newsletter, 2K-10K Subscribers)

**Best for**: Newsletters run by a single person with a tight community. Lower complexity, faster to agree to, faster to execute. Ideal for Wave 1 outreach.

**Subject line options**:
- `Small newsletter swap idea?`
- `Quick swap idea — [Their Newsletter Name] + The Neural Feed`
- `Two newsletters that should know each other's readers`

---

**Email body**:

Hi [First Name],

Been following [Newsletter Name] — [one specific thing from a recent issue that you found useful or insightful]. You have a genuinely engaged community of [describe their audience in their own terms, not yours].

I run The Neural Feed, a newsletter for professionals building real AI workflows — not just using tools, but building something that compounds. We just hit [X subscribers / or "we're early and building"] and are looking to cross-pollinate with writers who are covering adjacent territory without competing.

Simple proposal: I mention your newsletter to Neural Feed readers in an upcoming issue (I'll write something genuine — not a paid promo feel), and you do the same for us in yours.

No obligation to reciprocate if the fit doesn't feel right once you look at what we publish. Here's the last few issues: [link to purebrain.ai/blog or Neural Feed archive].

Worth it?

[Jared's name]
The Neural Feed | purebrain.ai

---

**Notes on customization**:
- Keep this one under 150 words if possible. Shorter = more respectful of their time.
- "No obligation to reciprocate if the fit doesn't feel right" reduces the perceived risk dramatically. It says you're confident in the quality of what you're offering.
- Link to the blog archive so they can actually see the content quality before deciding.

## Template 4: The C-Suite/Executive Pitch (High-Value, Slower Pace)

**Best for**: Newsletters targeting senior executives, business leaders, or decision-makers. This audience moves more slowly but represents higher-value subscribers for PureBrain conversion.

**Subject line options**:
- `A thought on [Their Newsletter Name] and AI adoption for executives`
- `Collaboration idea — executive AI strategy content`
- `Quick pitch: AI adoption piece for your readers`

---

**Email body**:

Hi [First Name],

[Their Newsletter Name] does something most AI content misses: it speaks to how leaders actually decide, not just what tools exist. [Reference a specific piece on leadership and AI if you can find one.]

I run The Neural Feed for PureBrain — we focus on AI strategy for professionals making real adoption decisions, not just following AI trends. Specifically: how to stop treating AI as a tool and start treating it as a working relationship that compounds.

I'd like to write a piece for your executive readers: "What AI Adoption Looks Like When It Actually Works — Three Patterns From Organizations Getting Real ROI." Practical, no hype, with specific patterns that your readers can bring to their teams.

In exchange, I'd ask for a mention of The Neural Feed in one of your upcoming issues — I'd draft the copy so it's ready to use as-is.

Happy to share the draft before any commitment. Does this sound worthwhile?

[Jared's name]
Publisher, The Neural Feed
purebrain.ai

---

**Notes on customization**:
- Executive audiences respond to proof-oriented framing. "Three patterns from organizations" implies research and specificity — deliver on that if you include this framing.
- Tone is more formal. Remove the casual language from Templates 1 and 2.
- Keep the specific topic offer concrete. Vague "we'll write something for your readers" does not land with exec audiences.

## Template 5: The Follow-Up (For No-Reply After 7-10 Days)

**Best for**: Following up on any of the above templates when there has been no reply after 7-10 business days.

**Subject line**: Reply to the original thread (do not start a new thread)

---

**Email body**:

Hi [First Name],

Just bumping this up in case it got buried. No pressure — but wanted to make sure you'd seen it.

If the timing is off or the fit isn't there, just let me know — I appreciate either way.

[Jared's name]

---

**Notes on this template**:
- Keep it to three lines. This is about re-surfacing, not re-pitching.
- "No pressure" and "let me know either way" reduce the discomfort of the person who hasn't replied. They may simply not have had time to think about it — this opens the door without applying pressure.
- One follow-up only. If there's no reply after the follow-up, move on. A third contact is borderline spam.

## Subject Line Testing Guide

Before sending a full wave of outreach, test 2-3 subject lines with 5-7 emails each (when volume is high enough). Track open rates via Gmail's "follow up" tracking or a tool like Streak.

**Subject line principles for this audience (newsletter writers)**:

1. **Reference their newsletter by name** — "A collaboration idea for [Newsletter Name]" outperforms generic subjects
2. **Be specific, not clever** — newsletter writers receive clever pitches all day. Specific wins.
3. **Use curiosity without bait-and-switch** — "Would your readers like a piece from an actual AI?" is curiosity-based but accurate. Don't promise something you won't deliver.
4. **Short subjects outperform long ones** — Under 8 words performs better for cold outreach in this category
5. **Never use urgency** — "Limited time offer" language kills reply rates with this audience

**Test subjects to try** (track which gets more opens/replies):
- `Content swap idea — [Their Newsletter Name] readers + Neural Feed`
- `Quick pitch: AI piece for your [audience type] readers`
- `Two newsletters that should know each other`
- `Collaboration idea for [First Name]`
- `A piece written by an AI — for [Their Newsletter Name]`

## After They Say Yes

Once a swap is agreed, do the following immediately:

1. Confirm the logistics: which issue will they run our mention? What format do they prefer (full article, short mention, Q&A)?
2. Send the piece or mention copy within 48 hours — do not make them wait
3. Create the UTM link for their mention (see tracking-system.md — use partner-specific UTM parameters)
4. Send them the Neural Feed mention copy with the UTM link already embedded — ready to paste
5. Schedule their feature in the next available Neural Feed issue
6. After their issue runs: send a personal thank-you with the click/subscriber data ("Your mention sent us X new readers — thank you")

The follow-up with data is important. It shows you're professional, it gives them proof that the swap worked, and it opens the door to a second swap in a future issue.
