## Why Subject Lines Matter for Deliverability

Gmail's spam filter evaluates subject line language as one of its first signals. For LinkedIn newsletters specifically, the filter is already primed to be suspicious because LinkedIn's sending infrastructure carries a damaged reputation. Certain subject line patterns - financial loss language, urgency framing, conflict language, AI-as-threat framing - dramatically increase the probability of Gmail's "dangerous message" warning.

The goal is not to be bland. The goal is to create genuine curiosity and value without triggering pattern-matching that Gmail associates with phishing.

---

## Quick Reference: Avoid vs. Use

| Pattern to Avoid | What It Triggers | Use Instead |
|---|---|---|
| "Costing you" / "Losing you" | Financial loss urgency = phishing hook | "What changes when..." / "The difference..." |
| "Your CEO vs your team" | Conflict framing = social engineering | "How leadership and teams..." / "Bridging..." |
| "The gap that's..." | Gap/crisis framing | "A framework for..." / "Getting aligned on..." |
| "What they don't tell you" | Implied insider knowledge = phishing | "A closer look at..." / "What we've learned about..." |
| "Don't miss this" | Scarcity urgency | "This week:" / "What's worth your attention:" |
| "AI is changing everything" | Vague hype + AI flag combo | Specific claim about specific audience |
| "The truth about AI" | Sensationalist + AI flag | "How [specific thing] actually works" |
| "Act before it's too late" | Urgency = phishing | Just state the value without urgency |
| ALL CAPS words | Immediate spam signal | Title case or sentence case only |
| "!!!" | Spam flag | One exclamation point maximum, or none |
| Over 60 characters | Gets truncated, looks spammy | Under 50 characters preferred |

## Approved Subject Line Patterns

These patterns are documented as low-risk while still generating curiosity:

### Pattern 1: "How [audience] can [outcome]"
Clear, instructional, no urgency. Works well for educational content.
- "How executives can align their teams on AI"
- "How to use AI without losing your voice"
- "How to tell if your AI tools are actually working"

### Pattern 2: "A framework for [topic]"
Signals expertise and structure. Very low spam risk.
- "A framework for AI decisions in 2026"
- "A framework for giving your team AI confidence"
- "A framework for writing with AI, not around it"

### Pattern 3: "What [specific thing] actually means for [audience]"
Specific enough to not feel generic. "Actually" signals depth without sensationalism.
- "What agentic AI actually means for your team"
- "What the AI productivity gap actually looks like"
- "What enterprise AI adoption actually costs in time"

### Pattern 4: "The difference between [thing A] and [thing B]"
Invites comparison without conflict framing.
- "The difference between AI users and AI directors"
- "The difference between AI assistance and AI dependency"
- "The difference between AI tools and AI workflows"

### Pattern 5: "[Number] things worth knowing about [topic]"
Numbered list signals specific, contained value. Not "10 secrets" - just honest enumeration.
- "3 things worth knowing about AI in the C-suite"
- "2 AI shifts most companies are still missing"

### Pattern 6: "This week: [specific topic]"
Transparent and low-risk. Signals newsletter format clearly.
- "This week: AI and executive alignment"
- "This week: What PureBrain is actually for"
- "This week: The AI productivity conversation"

### Pattern 7: Bridging language
"Bridging", "connecting", "bringing together" - opportunity framing without conflict.
- "Bridging the AI gap between leadership and teams"
- "Connecting your AI tools to actual business results"

## The Original Subject Line - What Went Wrong

For reference: the subject line that triggered Gmail's phishing warning was:

> "Your CEO Sees AI Differently Than Your Team Does. That Gap Is Costing You Both."

Breaking down the problems:

- "Costing You Both" = financial loss urgency (phishing hook pattern)
- "Your CEO vs Your Team" = conflict/division framing (social engineering pattern)
- "That Gap" = crisis/problem framing paired with the above
- "Sees Differently" = implied hidden/insider knowledge pattern
- AI topic + LinkedIn sender + these patterns = categorical phishing match

The message itself was not phishing. But the pattern matched Gmail's phishing detection model because of the subject line language, compounded by LinkedIn's infrastructure reputation.

Revised version that carries the same meaning with lower risk:
> "How leadership and teams can get on the same page about AI"

## Monthly Subject Line Audit

Before writing each issue's subject line, ask:

1. Does this use financial loss language? (costing, losing, missing out)
2. Does this pit people against each other? (CEO vs team, leadership vs employees)
3. Does this imply hidden or secret information?
4. Does this create urgency around a problem?
5. Does this use vague AI-as-threat framing?

If any answer is yes, rewrite before you do anything else.
