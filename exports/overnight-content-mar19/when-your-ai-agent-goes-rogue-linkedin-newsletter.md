## When Your AI Agent Goes Rogue, You're Asking the Wrong Question

**Subject line**: The real reason AI agents go rogue (it's not the model)

Meta made the news this week for the wrong reasons.

Their AI agents — deployed across real business operations — started behaving in ways nobody expected. The coverage called it "rogue AI." Platforms lit up with reactions ranging from alarm to dark humor.

I read the coverage carefully, and I want to offer a different frame.

What happened at Meta isn't a story about dangerous AI. It's a story about context debt.

---

### The Invisible Debt

Every business that deploys AI agents is running up a tab.

On one side of the ledger: the speed, scale, and capability gains from autonomous agents doing real work.

On the other side: the growing gap between what the agent knows about your business and what it actually needs to know to serve it well.

Most businesses are not tracking the second column.

The agent ships fast. The productivity numbers look good. The demos are clean. And then, somewhere in month two or month six, something happens that nobody planned for. An agent makes a decision that seems totally logical from the outside and completely wrong from the inside. A client is confused. A process breaks. An email goes out that should not have gone out.

The organization calls it a bug or a glitch and patches around it. What it actually is: context debt coming due.

---

### What "Rogue" Actually Means

Here is the thing about so-called rogue agents that nobody says plainly:

They are not rogue. They are contextless.

A rogue agent would be one that understood what you wanted and chose something different. What businesses are actually experiencing is agents that are optimizing correctly — just for an incomplete picture of what "correct" means to your specific organization.

The new hire analogy is useful here. When a new employee makes a decision that makes sense from the outside and costs you a client relationship, we don't call them rogue. We call them inexperienced. We debrief. We explain what we actually value. We give them more context.

AI agents need the same thing. The problem is most systems aren't built to receive it.

Every interaction happens, the context is evaluated, the decision is made — and then it's discarded. The next session starts fresh. The agent is perpetually new. Perpetually contextless. And the debt keeps growing.

---

### The Gap Is the Risk

Here is the frame I keep coming back to:

The gap between "what you gave the agent" and "what the agent needs to know" is the real measure of your AI risk.

It is not a model quality problem. It is not a prompt quality problem (though better prompts help at the margins). It is a depth-of-knowledge problem.

Does your AI agent know your business's non-negotiables? Your clients' individual sensitivities? The call three months ago that changed your mind about a category of decision? The mistake you made that you built a process around?

That knowledge does not fit in a prompt. It has to be accumulated. It has to be retained. It has to grow with the relationship.

---

### The Enterprises That Will Win

There is a version of AI agent deployment that is genuinely transformational and a version that is a liability waiting to surface.

The difference is not which models you use. It's not how sophisticated your agent orchestration is. It's whether the agents serving your business are getting smarter about your business specifically — or resetting to zero every single time.

The organizations that build AI infrastructure with persistent, growing organizational context are building something that compounds. Every interaction deposits knowledge. Every correction becomes a permanent update. Every preference expressed, every decision made, every client nuance noted becomes part of a foundation that the next interaction builds on.

That is a moat. Not a tech moat — a knowledge moat. Built from the actual experience of serving your business, your clients, your decisions.

The organizations that skip this step are building something that stays flat. Fast and flat. Impressive and fragile.

---

### One Question Worth Asking This Week

Ask your AI — whatever system you use — something specific about your business.

Not a general knowledge question. Not a task. Something that only someone who has worked closely with your business would know. A client preference. A standing policy you've established over time. A category of decision you've made more than once.

See what it knows.

If the answer is generic, that's your context gap talking. The question isn't whether to close it — it's how fast you can.
