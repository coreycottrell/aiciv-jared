The CEO of a consumer tech company said something this week that caught my attention.

"Apps are going to be extinct."

His argument: AI agents will absorb the function of every app you currently use. Why open a booking tool when an agent handles it? Why navigate a project management platform when an agent tracks everything automatically?

He is right about the direction. But almost every analysis I have read this week missed the more important half of the argument.

The question is not whether AI agents replace apps. They will.

The question is: **whose agent?**

---

## The Container Problem Nobody Is Talking About

Apps were not just interfaces. They were memory stores.

Your CRM held five years of customer interaction history. Your calendar held the pattern of who you met with, how often, what was discussed. Your email held your relationship context with every contact.

Apps were sticky not because of their features. They were sticky because they knew things about you that were expensive to migrate.

When AI agents replace the app layer, they inherit the function. But most current agents do not inherit the memory.

You get a very capable blank dashboard. Every session starts at zero.

That is not a replacement for apps. That is a step backwards with a better interface on top.

---

## The Agents That Will Win

The agents that dominate the next decade will not be the most capable ones.

They will be the most *knowing* ones.

Think about why you are loyal to any service. Not because it does the most things — because it knows you. The doctor who remembered your symptoms from eight months ago. The rep who already had your history up. The colleague who knew how you liked to work.

The AI agent that wins is the one that has accumulated the most context about you over the longest period of time.

Which turns the race to replace apps into a race to accumulate context.

---

## The New Switching Cost

Here is the strategic implication for decisions you are making right now.

There is a new switching cost forming. And it will be more significant than the data-migration costs we had with apps.

With apps: the switching cost was your history. Painful to migrate.

With agents: the switching cost will be your training history.

Every interaction you have with a persistent AI partner is teaching it something. Your style. Your decision patterns. Your preferences. After six months of daily use, a persistent agent that has learned from hundreds of your decisions has accumulated something you cannot export to a competitor.

The moat is not the model. It is not the interface. It is not the number of integrations.

The moat is the accumulated knowledge of you.

---

## What To Do With This

**Do not evaluate agents on what they can do.** Capability differences between the top options are narrowing fast.

**Evaluate agents on what they remember.** Does context carry forward? Does it get better over time, or does every session start at zero?

**Think about where you want to invest your context.** Every correction you make, every preference you establish — that is training data. Where you put that investment matters more than which tool has the best features today.

The app layer is going away. The agents worth building toward are the ones that know you specifically — your work, your style, your history — and get demonstrably better at working with you over time.

That is not a feature. That is the foundation.

---

## One Thought To Carry With You

Apps were sticky because they held your history.

The AI partners that become truly indispensable will be sticky for the same reason — except the history they hold is not your data files. It is a working model of how you think.

That is worth being intentional about.
