## The AI Relationship You Can't Take With You

When you switch AI tools, you lose more than your prompts.

Most companies do not think about this until it is too late. They evaluate AI platforms on model quality, pricing, integrations, and security posture. They make the switch. And on day one with the new tool, they discover that the eight months they spent working with the previous AI — the accumulated context, the learned preferences, the institutional patterns it developed about how their team works — is gone.

Not less functional. Gone.

This is the switching cost that does not appear in vendor RFPs. And it is becoming one of the most significant strategic considerations in enterprise AI in 2026.

---

**The research is consistent on this point.**

Workers who describe their AI tool as "knowing their work context well" report 42% higher satisfaction and 31% better output quality than workers using the same tools without that persistent context layer, according to the 2025 Salesforce State of Work report. The tool is identical. The context is not.

Gartner's 2026 Enterprise AI report named "AI relationship depth" — accumulated contextual advantage from long-running deployments — as an emerging competitive differentiator in knowledge-intensive industries. Companies that optimized for context retention over constant model chasing are pulling ahead on output quality.

The model matters less every quarter. The relationship matters more.

---

**Here is the structural problem.**

AI context is not like other enterprise data. Conversation logs and saved templates can usually be exported. But the deeper layer — the trained pattern of working together, the system's model of your preferences, the accumulated recognition of what lands well versus what you revise heavily — is not portable. There is no standard format. No import/export protocol. What develops inside a platform stays inside that platform.

This is a new kind of vendor lock-in. Not infrastructure lock-in in the traditional sense. Relational lock-in. The switching cost is not a migration fee. It is the cost of starting a relationship over from zero — and absorbing the productivity gap while you rebuild.

Most procurement teams are not evaluating for this. By the time it matters, the decision has already been made.

---

**Two questions worth adding to every AI vendor evaluation:**

One: How does this system accumulate contextual knowledge about how our team works, and how does that accumulate over time?

Two: What happens to that accumulated context if we stop using the platform?

If the vendor cannot answer both questions clearly, that gap tells you something important about whether they have actually built for the long-term relationship — or whether they are optimized for acquisition, not retention.

---

The frame that has shifted for me: the question is not which AI tool is best right now. The question is which AI relationship you want to be building two years from now.

That is a different evaluation. And it leads to different decisions.
