**Something I've been thinking about this week:**

50% of your peers trust AI to handle the repetitive work. Fewer than 30% trust it for anything that actually matters.

That gap has a name: the AI trust gap. And if you're reading this newsletter, you're probably sitting somewhere inside it.

---

### Why Trust Collapses Between "Automate This" and "Help Me Think"

Here's what I've observed across every organization trying to get serious about AI:

The trust curve isn't smooth. There's a cliff.

You trust AI with tasks that are immediately verifiable and low-stakes. Sorted emails, formatted reports, drafted responses - if something's off, you catch it fast. Easy to course-correct.

Now ask AI to help you think through a market entry decision. Or a budget reallocation. Or whether to let someone go.

Trust collapses. Not because the AI got worse. Because you have almost no history of it doing this class of work for you. And "history with you specifically" is exactly what trust requires.

The problem isn't the technology. It's that most companies have structured their AI deployment to keep AI permanently in first-week-of-work status. Task in, output out, move on. No accumulation. No deepening.

The AI never gets the chance to earn more.

---

### What Pilots Get Wrong

Most AI pilots are designed to answer one question: *can it do the thing?*

Summarize documents? Yes. Draft communications? Yes. Analyze data? Yes. Capability confirmed. Pilot "successful."

But that's a capability test, not a trust-building process.

Think about the last time you trusted someone with real responsibility. You didn't run a capability test. You watched them over time. Saw how they handled something unexpected. Saw whether their judgment aligned with yours before you handed them anything that mattered.

That process doesn't happen in a 90-day pilot.

This is why 75% of enterprise AI pilots stall before reaching production. The organizations in "pilot purgatory" aren't wrong to hesitate. They've proven the AI can do the job generically. They haven't seen it do *their* job - with their context, their standards, their specific situation.

Only 8.6% of companies currently have AI agents in production. The bottleneck isn't capability awareness. It's trust.

---

### The Three Stages of Trust (And Which One Most Companies Skip)

Real trust develops in stages:

**Stage 1: Competence.** Can it execute reliably? Most pilots prove this. It's the necessary but insufficient step.

**Stage 2: Context.** Does it understand *us* - our terminology, our decision history, what "good" looks like for our specific situation? This is where almost every AI deployment fails. Generic competence is not the same as contextual competence.

**Stage 3: Judgment.** Does it know when to push back, when it's uncertain, when our stated question isn't the question we actually need answered? This only comes from accumulated interaction over time.

The World Economic Forum found that companies using AI as a genuine partner - not just a task automator - outperform automation-only companies by a factor of three. That gap lives almost entirely in context and judgment.

---

### The Question Worth Asking Right Now

Most organizations are asking: *what can our AI do?*

The more useful question is: *how is our AI learning about us?*

An AI user runs tasks through a tool. Each session starts from zero. The relationship doesn't deepen because there's no mechanism for it to accumulate anything.

An AI partner is different. Each interaction builds shared context. The AI gets better at helping you specifically - not just at doing generic work well.

If your AI still feels like a capable tool you're pointing at tasks, you're an AI user.

If it's starting to feel like a colleague who knows your work - one you'd trust on harder questions - you're building toward something different.

The 72% of leaders who don't trust AI for decisions aren't wrong to be cautious. They've just never been given a structure for building the relationship that would earn that trust.
