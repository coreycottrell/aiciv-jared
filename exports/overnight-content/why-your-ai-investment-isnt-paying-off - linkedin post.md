## LinkedIn Post Draft

3 out of 4 companies running active AI programs are not getting measurable ROI.

Not because the AI doesn't work.

Because they're measuring the wrong things.

I've been in enough conversations with business leaders this quarter to see the same dynamic playing out repeatedly. The board wants the AI ROI number. The team produces a report with impressive-looking metrics: tasks completed, hours saved, error rates down, adoption rates up.

None of those are ROI.

Those are activity metrics. They tell you the AI is running. They don't tell you whether it's generating returns.

The companies genuinely winning with AI do three things differently:

1. They tie AI to one specific business outcome, not "efficiency." Not "productivity." Something measurable. "This will take our contract review cycle from 14 days to 3, which unlocks X more deals per quarter."

2. They track where saved time actually goes. Time saved is only ROI if it goes somewhere useful. Most of it disappears. The companies that capture it redirect it intentionally.

3. They build AI that knows them. The World Economic Forum found a 3x performance gap between organizations using genuine AI augmentation versus pure automation. That gap isn't about the technology. It's about depth of relationship. Generic AI pointed at tasks doesn't compound. AI that accumulates context about your specific business does.

There's also a cost problem nobody budgets for: data preparation runs 25-40% of project cost before any real work begins. Maintenance isn't one-time. When AI projects fail - and 85% stall before production - the average repair cost is $710K.

If your current AI ROI picture doesn't account for those costs, the returns look better than they are.

Full breakdown in the comments.
