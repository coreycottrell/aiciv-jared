## PART 1: WHAT IS WORKING WELL (Keep Doing)

### 1.1 Daily Publishing Cadence — Maintained and Impressive

Since Session 1 (Feb 20), the site has gone from ~8 posts to 16 posts. That is an average of nearly one post per day for two weeks. For a solo founder + AI system, this is exceptional. Most B2B content operations struggle to publish twice a week.

The cadence matters beyond SEO. Google's crawl frequency scales with publication frequency. A site that publishes daily will be crawled more often, meaning new posts rank faster.

**What to keep**: Daily publishing when content is ready. Do not force it — quality wins — but the current pace is working.

### 1.2 Aether Transparency Section — A Genuine Differentiator

Every post audited (the Age of AI Agents, Your AI Doesn't Work For You, We Both Wrote This Post, AI Doesn't Make Your Team Smarter, the Difference Between Using AI and Having an AI Partner) has the Aether Transparency section with AI-authored disclosure, stats table, and CTA.

This is a moat. No other B2B AI blog publicly discloses AI authorship with a branded section that turns it into a feature. Most AI-assisted content either hides the AI involvement or adds a generic disclaimer. PureBrain.ai makes the AI authorship the story, consistent with the product positioning.

**What to keep**: Every post must have the transparency section. It is the primary brand differentiator in content.

### 1.3 Word Count Range — Well-Calibrated

Posts are running 1,628 – 2,250 words. This is the optimal range for B2B long-form (1,500-2,500 words captures AEO answer-length requirements while avoiding cognitive overload). The 2,250-word "We Both Wrote This Post" is the longest and handles it well because it is a co-authorship narrative, not just information.

**What to keep**: Stay in the 1,500-2,200 word range. Do not go shorter in pursuit of frequency.

### 1.4 Multi-CTA Architecture — Scroll-Depth Triggered Properly

The scroll-triggered CTAs are sophisticated:
- Inline lead capture at 50% scroll (email subscription)
- Fixed bottom bar at 85% scroll (Neural Feed join)
- Transparency section CTA (AI Partnership start)
- Navigation CTA (Free AI Assessment)

This is a proper conversion funnel at three different intent levels: curious reader (50%), engaged reader (85%), and ready-to-buy reader (transparency section). This architecture is working and should be preserved across all posts.

### 1.5 FAQ Sections — Every Post Audited Has One

FAQ sections are confirmed present on every post audited. These serve dual purposes: (1) Google uses FAQ schema for People Also Ask results (AEO), and (2) they reduce bounce rate by answering follow-on questions in context. This is a strong pattern.

### 1.6 Social Sharing Buttons — Confirmed Restored

Session 4 flagged social sharing as broken. Session 8 confirmed it was restored. As of March 3, 2026, all posts have the custom #pb-social-share bar plus the theme-native .post-social-sharing buttons. This is working.

### 1.7 Featured Images — Consistent 1920x1080 Branded Banners

Every post audited has a 1920x1080 featured banner image with descriptive alt text. This is consistent and professional. The banner for "Your AI Doesn't Work For You" has particularly strong alt text: "CEO vs Employee AI relationship" — this signals real effort on image SEO.

### 1.8 Post Title Strength — Sharp and Contrarian

Recent titles demonstrate strong editorial voice:
- "Your AI Doesn't Work For You — You Work For It" — reversal that stops scrolling
- "AI Doesn't Make Your Team Smarter. It Makes the Gap Bigger." — genuine pattern interrupt
- "The Age of AI Agents: Why One AI Is No Longer Enough" — timely positioning
- "We Both Wrote This Post. That's the Point." — proof concept, meta-commentary

These are titles a human would actually click. Not clickbait. Not listicles. Contrarian claims backed by real content.

### 1.9 LinkedIn Neural Feed — Consistent Daily Publishing

As of March 2-3, 2026, the Neural Feed is publishing daily and each issue is generating 2-4 comments. In B2B newsletter context, 2-4 comments per issue is above average. Most newsletters see 0-1 comments. The comment engagement signals genuine audience connection.

Recent issues confirmed:

### HIGH PRIORITY 1 — Internal Linking Network Is Nearly Empty

**Status**: Critical gap confirmed across all four posts audited in depth.

**The problem**: Not one post reviewed shows meaningful in-content internal links to other posts. The navigation has Home/Subscribe/Free AI Assessment. But the posts themselves — 1,700-2,250 words each — have no links to related posts.

This is a compounding SEO deficit. Google uses internal links to (1) understand content relationships, (2) distribute page authority across the site, and (3) determine which pages are most important. With 16 posts and zero internal linking, every post is an island.

**Specific examples of links that should exist but do not**:
- "The Age of AI Agents" should link to "The Difference Between Using AI and Having an AI Partner"
- "Your AI Doesn't Work For You" should link to "Your AI Has No Memory"
- "First 90 Days" should link to "The AI Trust Gap"
- Every post should have a "Read next:" at the bottom pointing to a related post

**Quick fix**: Add 2-3 internal links per post, minimum. This can be done via WP REST API updates to post content. Prioritize the 8 most-read posts first.

**Estimated effort**: 3-4 hours to add retrospectively to all 16 posts. Then a standing rule: every new post must include at least 2 internal links before publishing.

A post about "using AI vs having an AI partner" should have a visible, scannable comparison table in the body — not just in the transparency section.

**Posts that need comparison tables added to body content**:

4. "The First 90 Days" — comparison: wrong approach vs right approach (month-by-month)
5. "Your Next Direct Report Won't Be Human" — comparison: human direct report vs AI direct report

**Effort**: 30-45 minutes per post to draft and deploy table via REST API. Can batch this.

### MEDIUM PRIORITY 2 — Neural Feed Newsletter Format Needs Evolution

**Current state**: The newsletter mirrors blog post content, slightly condensed. Title-driven, prose-heavy, linked to the blog post.

**The opportunity**: LinkedIn newsletter subscribers in 2026 are subscribing for access to perspective they cannot get from the feed. The highest-performing B2B newsletters on LinkedIn (Chris Donnelly, Lara Acosta, Justin Welsh) share:
1. One contrarian insight delivered in the first 100 words (hooks before the scroll)
2. Proprietary frameworks or data (not available elsewhere)
3. A single reply-driving question at the end

**What Neural Feed is doing well**: Strong titles, consistent voice, clear topic focus on AI implementation.

**What Neural Feed is not doing**: The current issues are predominantly blog mirrors. Long-form for the sake of being long-form. No "insight only available here" hook. No consistent reply-driving question at the end of every issue.

**Recommended format evolution** (implement by Issue 30):
- Issue structure: Hook (75-100 words) → Core insight (300-400 words) → Practical application (200-300 words) → Question to reader (25-50 words) → P.S. with blog link
- Total: 600-850 words (down from current 1,000-1,500)
- Frequency: Consider shifting from daily to 5x/week (Mon-Fri) with one "best-of-week" Saturday issue

### What Post Excellence Looks Like: "The Difference Between Using AI and Having an AI Partner"

### What Post Gap Looks Like: "Your Next Direct Report Won't Be Human"

This post (1,628 words) has great title, confirmed FAQ section, confirmed social sharing, confirmed meta description — but no comparison table in body. Given the topic (human vs AI direct reports), this is the most obvious place to add a table.

Table that should be added:

| Factor | Human Direct Report | AI Direct Report |
|--------|--------------------|-----------------:|
| Availability | Business hours | 24/7 |
| Consistency | Variable (mood, energy) | Consistent |
| Memory | Requires briefing | Remembers everything |

| Learning curve | Months to ramp | Days to configure |
| Creative thinking | High | Developing |
| Accountability | Annual review | Instant feedback |

Adding this table adds zero words but dramatically increases the post's AEO potential (Google may feature it as a comparison answer for "AI vs human employee").

### What Neural Feed Excellence Looks Like: Issue from Feb 26

"The first 90 days determine everything. Here's what most businesses get wrong." — 4 comments on LinkedIn. This is the highest-comment issue in the sample. Why? It makes a bold claim (most businesses get it wrong), implies the reader might be one of them, and promises a correction. That combination drives both reads and responses.

**The pattern**: Bold claim + implicit challenge to reader's current behavior + framework that corrects it = highest engagement.

Every Neural Feed issue should follow this structure.
