## Part 2: First 30 Minutes After Posting — The Engagement Sprint

This is the most important 30-minute window in LinkedIn. The algorithm measures initial engagement velocity to decide distribution. Here is the exact protocol:

### Minutes 0–5: Post + First Comment

1. **Post the stat card** (stat-card-2-market.png + copy from content calendar)
2. **Immediately post the blog link as the first comment** — do not include the link in the post itself
   - Comment text: `Full analysis → [blog URL] — why the companies that aren't yet building agent systems will find the gap structural, not catchable.`
3. **Do not edit the post** after publishing — edits reset the algorithm clock

### Minutes 5–15: Pre-warm Engagement

Before the algorithm decides your distribution, you need signal. Go engage on other people's posts NOW (not after yours gets traction).

**Comment on 5 posts from this priority list** (in this order):
1. Anyone who commented on Day 1 or Day 2 posts — reply to them if you haven't
2. Active AI/enterprise posts from the past 12–24 hours
3. Relevant VC or CIO posts on AI investment, digital transformation

**Comment quality rule** (from KB — 15+ word comments get 2.5x reach boost):
- Add a data point not in your own post
- Raise a genuine tension or follow-on question
- Never: "Great post!" / "Totally agree" / generic agreement

Example angle for AI agent posts:
> "The deployment gap you're describing is real. What we're seeing is that organizations treating agents like software rollouts (one-time implementation) are not capturing the compounding — the value accrues to agents that have been running and learning. Static deployment = static advantage."

### Minutes 15–30: Reply Sprint on Your Post

Once you have your first 2–3 comments, reply to each one. LinkedIn algorithm sees reply threads as engagement multiplier.

**Reply strategy**:
- Add a NEW data point not in the original post to every reply
- Ask one genuine follow-on question per reply
- Keep replies 2–4 sentences — long enough to show thought, short enough to invite another reply

**Example reply patterns for this post**:
- To someone questioning the timeline: "The METR data is actually conservative — they measure task completion on a standardized benchmark. Real-world operational autonomy is developing faster because agents in production environments have training signal the benchmark doesn't capture. The gap between benchmark performance and operational value is widening."
- To someone who tags a colleague: "Appreciate the tag — [Name], curious whether your team is seeing the institutional knowledge accumulation dynamic in your portfolio companies yet."

### Who to Tag / Notify (Selective — Not Mass-Tag)

**Do not mass-tag people.** The algorithm detects this and penalizes posts. Tag only people where it is genuinely relevant.

For today's $52.6B market stat card, consider tagging 1–2 people max:
- A VC or investor contact who would find the market sizing genuinely useful
- A portfolio company contact who has been discussing AI investment timelines

Tag in the first comment, not the post itself. Pattern: "Tagging [Name] — your framing on AI infrastructure timing from [their post] connects directly to this."

## Algorithm Notes (Why This Protocol Works)

From the knowledge base (Richard van der Blom, 1.8M+ posts; 2026 data):

1. **First-hour engagement velocity** is the strongest distribution signal. Posts that hit ~15+ meaningful interactions in hour 1 get pushed to 2nd-degree connections. This is why the pre-warm engagement (commenting on others before you post) matters — it signals to people who will see your post within minutes of publishing.

2. **Comment threading** (replies to comments) is a multiplier. Each reply counts as an additional engagement event. A post with 10 comments and 10 replies from the author looks like 20 engagement signals.

3. **Reply quality** matters. LinkedIn's AI classifies comment content. Substantive replies (15+ words with new information) score higher than "Thanks for sharing!"

4. **Dwell time** is the hidden metric. The stat cards are well-optimized for this — data-dense, requires a moment to read. More than a text post, image posts where people stop to read the visual boost dwell time.

5. **The "link in comments" convention** is now understood by the algorithm. LinkedIn no longer penalizes first-comment links the way it did links in the post body. The first comment link is now the standard for long-form drivers.
