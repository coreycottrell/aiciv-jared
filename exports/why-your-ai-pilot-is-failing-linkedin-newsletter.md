Hey [First Name],

I need to tell you something that most AI consultants won't say:

Your pilot is probably in purgatory right now. And there's a decent chance no one at your company is willing to admit it.

---

### What I'm Seeing

I've had conversations with CTOs and CIOs across mid-market companies over the past several months. There's a pattern that comes up almost every single time.

They describe an AI initiative that started with real momentum. Executive sponsorship. Budget. A vendor promising transformation.

Then I ask: "Is it in production?"

The pause before the answer tells me everything.

"We're in late-stage testing."

"We're finalizing the rollout plan."

"We've had some data challenges we're working through."

Translation: it stalled. Months ago. And the team is running out of ways to describe why.

---

### The Numbers (That Should Make Every CIO Uncomfortable)

95% of enterprise AI pilots fail to deliver measurable ROI [CLAIM:1].

For every 33 pilots launched, only 4 reach production [CLAIM:2].

61% of companies' data isn't actually ready for AI when the pilot starts [CLAIM:3].

And only 1 in 5 companies has mature AI agent governance in place [CLAIM:4].

I'm not sharing these to pile on. I'm sharing them because if your pilot is struggling, these numbers mean you're not uniquely failing - you're experiencing a systemic problem that the industry created by selling the technology before the organizational readiness existed.

---

### The Part That Changes Everything

Here's what McKinsey found when they looked at the 5% of companies that actually succeeded [CLAIM:5]:

AI success is 70% people, process, and culture. 20% data and technology. 10% algorithms.

Most AI budgets invert that completely.

We've been buying solutions to a technology problem that was never really a technology problem.

And here's the piece that hit me hardest when I first read it:

HBR found that employees with high anxiety about AI use it 65% of the time - but defensively [CLAIM:6]. Not to do better work. To protect themselves. To have something to point to.

That's not transformation. That's compliance theater.

You cannot automate your way past a team that's afraid of the tool you just deployed.

---

### What the Companies Escaping Purgatory Are Doing Differently

They're not finding a better AI vendor. They're changing their approach to the whole problem.

Three specific things I've seen work:

**They define "production" before the pilot starts.** Not "successful demo." Not "internal adoption." What does it look like when this is deployed, integrated, and generating the return we promised leadership?

**They budget for the 70%.** Change management, training, workflow redesign - these aren't line items to cut when the project goes over budget. For the companies succeeding, they're the primary investment.

**They kill stalled pilots fast.** Not every pilot deserves 18 months. If it's been stuck at the same phase for 90 days, either fix the blocker this week or reallocate the resources. The sunk cost fallacy is quietly destroying AI ROI across the industry.

---

### My Take

The AI industry sold transformation before it sold readiness.

That's created a generation of pilots that were designed, consciously or not, to stay in demo mode. Safe. Impressive. Never quite shipping.

The companies that break through aren't necessarily the ones with the best technology. They're the ones with leaders who are willing to have the uncomfortable conversation about what's actually blocking deployment - and whether it's a data problem, an integration problem, or a culture problem they've been calling a data problem.

That conversation is hard. It's also the only one that leads out of purgatory.
