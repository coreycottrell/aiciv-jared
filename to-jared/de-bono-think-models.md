## SECTION 1: CREATIVE THINKING TOOLS (Lateral Thinking)

### 1. Lateral Thinking

**What it is:** Moving "laterally" across brain patterns instead of deeper down familiar paths. The brain forms self-organising patterns that are asymmetric - what's obvious in hindsight is NOT reachable by logic in foresight. Lateral thinking provides methods to escape dominant patterns and reach side tracks.

**How to use it:**
1. Identify the dominant pattern (the obvious answer everyone goes to)
2. Use one of the lateral tools (random word, provocation, challenge) to escape that pattern
3. Move to a new "side track" - something that makes sense in hindsight even if it wasn't reachable directly
4. Evaluate the new position for value

**The core logic:** A tree trunk splits into branches. An ant on the trunk reaching a specific leaf has 1:8,000 odds. But an ant ON that leaf reaching the trunk has 100% odds. Asymmetry means: creative ideas are obvious in hindsight but inaccessible by forward logic. You need tools to arrive at the leaf from the "wrong" direction.

**Prompting application:**
> "Don't work from the obvious starting point. Use lateral thinking - approach this problem from a completely different starting point and work backwards. What's obvious in hindsight that forward logic would never reach?"

---

### 2. Random Word Tool (Random Entry)

**What it is:** Using a completely unrelated random noun as a starting point to break out of the dominant pattern. The random word is NOT the idea - it opens a new entry route into the idea space. Generated 21,000 ideas in a single afternoon at a steel company.

**How to use it:**
1. Define your focus clearly (what you want ideas about)
2. Select a random noun (pick from a clock's second hand, random page of a dictionary, etc.)
3. List attributes, associations, or aspects of the random word
4. Use each association as a new entry point into your focus - look for connections, analogies, transfers
5. The more remote the word seems, the more powerful the result

**Example:** Focus = "restaurant ideas." Random word = "cinema."
- Cinemas are dark → dark restaurant concept → private booths
- Cinemas have set times → timed menus, choose by time not dish
- Cinemas offer entertainment → DVD/earphone dining
- Cinemas sell concessions → late-night snack bar concept

**Prompting application:**
> "Random word: [WORD]. Use this as a random entry point into [FOCUS AREA]. What does [WORD] suggest that could apply to [FOCUS AREA]? Don't look for logical connection - use it as a peripheral starting point."

Or more directly:
> "Generate ideas about [TOPIC] using random word entry. Start from the periphery with the concept of [RANDOM CONCEPT] and work inward."

---

### 3. Challenge

**What it is:** Blocking the obvious dominant path - not as criticism, but as deliberate exploration. Challenge acknowledges "this may be the best way - but we block it temporarily to seek alternatives." Works even on ideas that are already excellent.

**How to use it:**
1. Identify an assumption, method, or convention that is "taken for granted"
2. State: "We are going to challenge this - not because it's wrong, but to look for alternatives"
3. Generate alternatives as if the challenged element didn't exist or was done differently
4. Shell Oil challenge example: oil wells were drilled vertically for 80+ years. Challenge: "Why vertical?" Result: horizontal drilling now produces 3-6x more oil.

**Critical rule:** Challenge is NEVER an attack. You can challenge even the best ideas. This is what allows you to improve things that aren't broken.

**Prompting application:**
> "Challenge the assumption that [ELEMENT]. Don't criticize it - just temporarily block it and explore: what alternatives exist if we didn't have/do this? What would we do instead?"

> "What are the assumptions embedded in [APPROACH]? Challenge each one - not because they're wrong but to open alternatives."

---

### 4. Provocation (Po)

**What it is:** A deliberate impossible or illogical statement used as a mental stepping stone. The word "po" signals a provocation - something that should NOT be judged as right or wrong but used for its "movement value." Named from Provocative Operation, also linked to "possible," "hypothesis," "pose."

**Why it works:** Self-organising systems (like the brain) get locked in "local equilibrium." Mathematicians call the escape process "annealing" - you heat steel to break its crystal lock and reforge it stronger. Provocation disrupts the local equilibrium.

**Five types of provocation:**
1. **Escape** - Drop something taken for granted. "Po, watchdogs don't bark" → dog presses alarm button instead
2. **Reversal** - Reverse a normal relationship. "Po, filters add something instead of remove" → pinhole adds air, dilutes smoke
3. **Distortion** - Change a sequence or relationship. "Po, you speak before you dial" → voice-activated dialing; rehearsal recorder
4. **Exaggeration** - Exaggerate any dimension up or down. "Po, telephone calls can only last 2 minutes" → message compression technology
5. **Wishful Thinking** - "Wouldn't it be nice if..." "Po, the factory is downstream of itself" → legislation requiring intake downstream of output

**Key rule:** A provocation is NEVER a half-idea or improvement desire. "Po, trains were always on time" is weak - not surprising enough. "Po, you get paid for waiting for a train" is strong.

**Prompting application:**
> "Po: [impossible statement about the topic]. Now use movement from this provocation. Don't judge it - extract concepts, focus on differences, find positive aspects, or imagine it in action step by step."

> "Generate five provocations about [TOPIC] - one using escape, one reversal, one distortion, one exaggeration, one wishful thinking. Then move from the most surprising one."

---

### 5. Movement

**What it is:** The mental operation of moving FORWARD from an idea (including bad/wrong/impossible ones) instead of judging it. Where judgment says "accept or reject," movement says "where can I go FROM here?" It's the engine that turns provocations into valuable ideas.

**Five movement techniques:**
1. **Extract the Concept** - What concept is operating here? Use that concept elsewhere.
2. **Focus on the Difference** - What's different between the provocation and the current way? Build on those differences.
3. **Positive Aspects** - What values are immediately present? Use those values in a more practical form.
4. **Moment to Moment** - Imagine the provocation in action, frame by frame. Watch what happens.
5. **Special Circumstances** - Are there specific circumstances where this provocation has direct value?

**Example - Square wheels:**
- Movement applied: imagine the wheel rolling - it rises on a point, descends to flat, cyclically and predictably
- Concept extracted: anticipatory suspension
- Result: sensor-controlled suspension system that reads the road ahead and pre-adjusts

**Prompting application:**
> "Use movement (not judgment) on this idea: [IDEA]. Don't accept or reject it. Instead: (1) extract the underlying concept, (2) identify what's different from the norm and build on it, (3) find any positive aspects, (4) imagine it running step by step - what emerges?"

### 6. Concept Extraction

**What it is:** Identifying the underlying concept behind any idea or practice, then finding NEW ways to deliver that same concept. An idea is a specific implementation; a concept is the principle it serves.

**How to use it:**
1. Look at an existing idea, product, or practice
2. Ask: "What concept is this serving?" (operational, value, purpose, or descriptive concept)
3. State the concept at multiple levels from broad to specific
4. Generate alternative implementations of each concept level

**Example - Car insurance:**
- Idea: car insurance policy
- Concept: "protection against financial risk from car incidents"
- Alternative delivery: insurance tax added to petrol cost (everyone covered automatically)

**Example - Restaurants:**
- Concept extracted: "eating what you can purchase at that moment"
- Alternatives: phone-a-cook service, home delivery, dinner party marketplace

**Concept levels example (bicycle):**
- Very broad: means of transport
- Next: personal means of transport
- Next: human-powered, non-polluting transport
- Specific: two-wheeled, pedal-crank mechanism
- Each level generates different alternatives (roller blades, skateboards, inside-a-wheel vehicle...)

**Prompting application:**
> "Extract the underlying concept from [IDEA/PRACTICE] at three levels: broad, mid, and specific. Then generate alternative ways to deliver each concept level."

### 7. Concept Fan

**What it is:** A cascading structure from purpose/goal down through broad concepts to specific concepts to concrete ideas. Works backwards from the goal to multiply alternatives at each level. The broader the "broad concepts" are kept, the more powerfully the fan multiplies.

**How to use it:**
1. Write the PURPOSE/GOAL on the right side of a page
2. Generate 3-4 BROAD CONCEPTS (directions) that would serve that purpose - keep these genuinely broad
3. For each broad concept, generate SPECIFIC CONCEPTS that could serve it
4. For each specific concept, generate CONCRETE IDEAS
5. The cascade effect multiplies: 4 broad x 3 specific x 3 ideas = 36 possible routes

**Example - Traffic congestion:**
- Goal: deal with traffic congestion
- Broad concepts: reduce traffic / improve flow / increase travel space
- Under "reduce traffic": restrict vehicles / discourage traffic / reduce need to travel / multi-user vehicles
- Under "restrict vehicles": high entry tolls / purchase permission auction (Singapore) / special access licenses

**Prompting application:**
> "Build a concept fan for this goal: [GOAL]. Create 3-4 broad concept directions, then for each generate 3 specific concepts, then for each specific concept generate 2-3 concrete ideas. Keep the broad concepts as broad as possible."

---

### 8. Focus (Purpose Focus vs. Area Focus)

**What it is:** Defining precisely WHERE you want new ideas before using any lateral thinking tools. Without focus, creativity is random. Two types of focus serve different purposes.

**Purpose Focus:** You know why you want ideas - there's a problem to solve, a task to achieve, a conflict to resolve. Like knowing your destination.

**Area Focus:** You just define the area of interest without a specific purpose. "I want ideas about wine glasses" - any kind of idea about wine glasses. Random word tool works especially well here.

**The Creative Hit List:** A formal list of 10 or so focuses that need creative attention, displayed publicly (bulletin board, website, desk cards). Items can be purpose or area focuses. Creates permanent demand for creative effort. Team members can be assigned specific items with deadlines.

**Prompting application:**
> "I want to use lateral thinking on this focus: [FOCUS]. This is an area focus - any ideas welcome. Generate ideas using random entry."

> "Maintain a creative hit list for [PROJECT/BUSINESS]. What are the 10 most important areas needing new ideas right now?"

### 9. Septine (NEW - First introduced in this book)

**What it is:** Seven scattered thoughts about a situation, read slowly, to sensitize different areas of the brain. Not an analysis - a scatter. The 7 points activate different neural clusters that sensitize connected clusters, allowing unexpected ideas to emerge.

**How to use it:**
1. Think of your topic/situation
2. Write down 7 different, scattered thoughts - single words, phrases, or short sentences
3. No logical sequence required - scatter is the point
4. Don't try to analyze or connect them
5. Read them slowly, over and over
6. Notice what ideas or clarity emerges

**Example Septine on "holidays":**
- change / low hassle / relax / interest / different / comfort / travel
- Idea emerged: holiday at home with an agency-provided "interesting house guest" - all the interest and comfort, no travel

**The brain mechanism:** Neural clusters activate, tire, and transfer activity to adjacent sensitized clusters. The 7 points activate non-adjacent clusters simultaneously, creating unusual sensitizations that produce unexpected connections.

**Prompting application:**
> "Create a septine on [TOPIC]: give me 7 scattered, non-sequential points - words or short phrases - covering very different aspects. Then read them together and see what ideas emerge."

> "I'm going to give you a septine: [7 words]. Read these as a scatter, not a sequence. What ideas or clarifications emerge from holding all 7 simultaneously?"

## SECTION 2: EXPLORATORY THINKING (Six Thinking Hats)

### 10. Parallel Thinking Framework

**What it is:** The alternative to argument for exploring a subject. Instead of A attacking B's position, everyone moves to the SAME position together and looks from there, then moves together to the next position. Like all walking to the south side of a building, then east, then north.

**Why argument fails for exploration:**
- Destructive and negative, only attack
- No generative energy - can't produce new ideas
- 5% weakness destroys 95% strength
- Ego drives winning over truth
- Skilled arguer beats better case
- Not designed to explore - only to destroy

**What parallel thinking provides:**
- Everyone examines same dimension together
- All contribute to same direction
- No adversarial dynamic
- Much faster (10-minute decisions vs. 30-day meetings at ABB)
- Surfaces real information and honest assessment

**Prompting application:**
> "Use parallel thinking on [TOPIC]. Don't argue. Instead, let's all look in the same direction together: first at information, then at risks, then at benefits, then at creativity."

---

### 11. Six Thinking Hats System

**What it is:** Designed 1984. A system of 6 colored hats representing 6 modes of thinking. Worn metaphorically - everyone wears the SAME hat at the same time. Separates emotional, logical, creative, and critical modes to prevent confusion and maximize each mode.

**Why 6 hats work:** The zebra analogy - when a zebra hears a rustle, brain chemicals sensitize all danger-processing circuits. If you try to do everything at once, you operate purely in negative/defensive mode. The hats separate modes so each can operate fully.

---

### 12. Blue Hat - Process Control

**What it is:** The "conductor of the orchestra" hat. Concerned with thinking ABOUT the thinking process, not the subject itself. Used at start (define focus, set agenda, choose hat sequence) and end (outcomes, summary, next steps). Bookend hat.

**Key functions:**
- What focus are we working on?
- What sequence of hats should we use?
- How much time per hat?
- What is the outcome we need?
- What have we concluded?

**Prompting application:**
> "Wear the Blue Hat: What should the focus of our thinking be? What sequence of thinking modes makes sense? What outcome do we need from this session?"

> "Blue hat summary: Based on everything explored, what is the conclusion and what are the next steps?"

---

### 13. White Hat - Information

**What it is:** Neutral, objective information. Facts, data, figures. Questions about what information is missing. No opinions, no interpretations. If conflicting information exists, both versions are listed in parallel without argument - to be resolved when needed.

**Key questions:**
- What information do we have?
- What information is missing?
- What information would we like to have?
- How do we get the missing information?

**Prompting application:**
> "White hat only: What are the facts, figures, and data? What information is missing? No opinions or interpretations - just what is known and what isn't."

---

### 14. Red Hat - Feelings and Intuition

**What it is:** Feelings, emotions, intuitions, hunches. In normal discussion these must be disguised as logic to be acceptable. Under the red hat they exist as valid input without justification or explanation. Period is brief - just surfaces what's there.

**Key invitation:**
- "What is your gut feeling?"
- "What emotions does this bring up?"
- "What does your intuition say?"
- No need to justify - feelings are valid data

**Prompting application:**
> "Red hat: What's the gut feeling here? No justification needed. What does intuition say about this, even if it can't be logically supported?"

---

### 15. Black Hat - Critical Thinking

**What it is:** Judgment, criticism, caution, risk assessment. What's wrong? What are the dangers? Why won't it work? All the negative thinking concentrated here rather than scattered throughout. "Possibly the most useful of all hats" - but has its defined, bounded place.

**Key questions:**
- What are the risks and dangers?
- What could go wrong?
- What are the weaknesses?
- Why won't this work?
- What does precedent suggest about failure?

**Prompting application:**
> "Black hat only: What are the risks, weaknesses, dangers, and reasons this might fail? Don't offer solutions - just surface all the negatives."

---

### 16. Yellow Hat - Value and Optimism

**What it is:** Positive thinking, value-seeking, benefits. Requires deliberate effort and skill - much harder than black hat thinking. "Value sensitivity" - the ability to find value in ANYTHING, even things you don't like and won't use. Without this, creative ideas get killed before being explored.

**Key questions:**
- What are the benefits?
- What value does this create?
- Under what circumstances could this work?
- How could it be done?
- What is genuinely good here?

**Prompting application:**
> "Yellow hat: Deliberately find value and benefits - even in ideas you don't personally favor. What could be good about this? What value might it create? Under what circumstances does it work well?"

---

### 17. Green Hat - Creativity

**What it is:** Creative effort, new ideas, alternatives, possibilities, modifications. When the green hat is on, participants are expected to make creative effort or stay quiet - the pressure to avoid silence drives creativity. May involve deliberate lateral thinking tools.

**Key prompts:**
- What are alternative approaches?
- What new ideas does this suggest?
- How could this be modified or improved?
- What possibilities exist beyond the obvious?

**Prompting application:**
> "Green hat: Generate new ideas, alternatives, and possibilities. Use lateral thinking if needed. What hasn't been considered? What modifications might improve this? Push beyond the obvious."

---

### 18. Six Hats Sequence Examples

**For exploring a new idea:**
Blue (set focus) → White (what do we know?) → Yellow (benefits) → Black (risks) → Green (improvements) → Blue (summary)

**For making a decision:**
Blue → White → Red (gut feels) → Black (risks) → Yellow (benefits) → Blue (decision)

**For solving a problem:**
Blue → White (current situation) → Green (new ideas) → Yellow (what has value) → Black (risks of solutions) → Blue (next steps)

**Prompting application:**
> "Run a Six Hats session on [TOPIC] using this sequence: [SEQUENCE]. I'll respond to each hat in turn."

## SECTION 3: PERCEPTUAL THINKING TOOLS (CoRT Programme)

### 19. PMI - Plus, Minus, Interesting

**What it is:** A structured attention-direction tool. Forces deliberate scanning of three dimensions of any idea or situation. The most basic perceptual tool. NOT a creative tool - a clarity tool.

**Key insight from research:** 30 Australian 12-year-olds unanimously agreed paying students for school attendance was a good idea. After using PMI, 29 of 30 changed their minds. The PMI revealed consequences they hadn't considered - not through teacher intervention but through structured scanning.

**How to use it:**
1. **Plus**: What are all the positive aspects? Benefits? Advantages?
2. **Minus**: What are all the negative aspects? Drawbacks? Disadvantages?
3. **Interesting**: What is worth noting even if not clearly positive or negative?

**Prompting application:**
> "Do a full PMI on [IDEA/PROPOSAL]:
> Plus - all positive aspects and benefits
> Minus - all negative aspects and drawbacks
> Interesting - things worth noting that are neither clearly positive nor negative"

---

### 20. CAF - Considering All Factors

**What it is:** A deliberate effort to survey ALL relevant factors before making a judgment or decision. Most thinking is driven by factors that "pull" attention. CAF forces attention to factors that aren't shouting for attention.

**How to use it:**
1. List the obvious factors (these are already pulling your attention)
2. Deliberately ask: what other factors should be considered?
3. Who else is affected? What else is involved? What are the less obvious dependencies?
4. What factors are often forgotten?

**Prompting application:**
> "CAF on [DECISION/SITUATION]: What are ALL the factors that should be considered? List the obvious ones first, then dig for the less obvious - stakeholders, second-order effects, hidden dependencies, often-forgotten elements."

---

### 21. C&S - Consequences and Sequels

**What it is:** Deliberate mapping of consequences across time horizons. Most people think they "consider consequences" but rarely do it systematically. Research: 86% of senior female executives favored paying women 15% more than men. After C&S: 15% favored it. The tool surfaces consequences that attitude alone doesn't catch.

**Four time horizons:**
1. **Immediate consequences** - right now, in the short term
2. **Short-term consequences** - within months
3. **Medium-term consequences** - 1-5 years
4. **Long-term consequences** - 5+ years, lasting effects

**Prompting application:**
> "C&S on [ACTION/DECISION]:
> Immediate consequences (days/weeks):
> Short-term consequences (months):
> Medium-term consequences (1-5 years):
> Long-term consequences (5+ years and lasting):"

---

### 22. AGO - Aims, Goals, Objectives

**What it is:** A tool to make explicit and clear what we are actually trying to achieve. Most thinking has only vague unstated goals. AGO forces specificity. Difference between having something "in the back of the mind" and actually spelling it out.

**Three levels:**
- **Aims**: The broad directional purpose - where are we generally heading?
- **Goals**: More specific targets - what would success look like?
- **Objectives**: Concrete measurable outcomes - what specifically must be achieved?

**Prompting application:**
> "AGO on [PROJECT/TASK]:
> Aim (broad purpose):
> Goals (specific success conditions):
> Objectives (concrete, measurable outcomes):"

---

### 23. FIP - First Important Priorities

**What it is:** Directing attention to what matters most. Not everything deserves equal attention. FIP forces identification of what must be done or considered FIRST, and what is most important among competing factors.

**How to use it:**
1. List all relevant factors or tasks
2. Identify which are most important (not just most urgent)
3. Identify what must be done first (order of operations)
4. Concentrate effort on FIP items

**Prompting application:**
> "FIP on [SITUATION/LIST]: Given everything we're considering, what are the first important priorities? What must be addressed first, and what matters most? Rank the top 3-5."

---

### 24. APC - Alternatives, Possibilities, Choices

**What it is:** Deliberately multiplying options. Most thinking stops at the first satisfactory option. APC forces exploration of alternatives even when you think you have the answer.

**Three dimensions:**
- **Alternatives**: Different ways to achieve the same end - alternatives of approach
- **Possibilities**: Things that might be true, might work, might be relevant - possibilities of explanation and perception
- **Choices**: The available options - what can we actually choose between?

**Prompting application:**
> "APC on [SITUATION]:
> Alternatives (different approaches):
> Possibilities (what might be true or possible):
> Choices (what can actually be chosen):"

---

### 25. OPV - Other People's Views

**What it is:** Getting inside the minds of other parties to understand their actual thinking - not what they SHOULD be thinking, but what they MIGHT be thinking given their perspective, needs, and fears. Reduced inter-tribal mine fights from 210/month to 4.

**Critical distinction:** OPV is not about what others should think (moralizing) or what you WANT them to think. It's an honest effort to see the world from their actual position.

**How to use it:**
1. Identify all parties involved
2. For each party: What are their needs? Their fears? Their hopes? Their constraints?
3. From their position: How does this situation look?
4. What would be important to them that you might be ignoring?

**Prompting application:**
> "OPV on [SITUATION]: For each stakeholder ([LIST THEM]), genuinely try to see the situation from their actual perspective. What are their needs, fears, and constraints? How does this look to them? What matters to them that I might be missing?"

---

### 26. Flowscape

**What it is:** A visual perceptual map based on "water logic" (flow) rather than "rock logic" (fixed categories). Each element has one arrow pointing to the element your mind naturally flows to next. Shows which elements are truly central vs. peripheral in your perception.

**How to build one:**
1. List all key elements in the situation (8-15 items)
2. For each element, ask: "If I'm thinking about THIS, what does my mind most naturally move to next?"
3. Draw one (and only one) arrow from each element to its successor
4. Elements can receive many arrows but emit only one
5. Look for clusters, loops, and peripheral/central distinctions

**What it reveals:** Elements you thought were central may be peripheral. You may find reinforcing loops. You see how your current perception is actually structured vs. how you thought it was.

**Prompting application:**
> "Build a flowscape for [SITUATION]. List 10-12 key elements, then for each one, where does the mind most naturally flow next? Draw the one-arrow connections. What clusters emerge? What's truly central?"

## SECTION 4: DESIGN THINKING

### 27. Design vs. Judgment Thinking

**What it is:** The fundamental distinction de Bono makes throughout the book. Judgment looks at what IS (with reference to the past). Design creates what COULD BE (with reference to the future). Judgment seeks truth. Design seeks value.

**When to use each:**
- **Judgment**: When you need to assess, evaluate, decide between known options, diagnose
- **Design**: When you need to create a way forward, solve a conflict, build something new, improve something that's working

**The conflict example:** Traditional approach to conflict = identify the "bad guys," condemn them, punish them. Design approach = look at the needs, fears, and greed of BOTH sides and design a way forward that accounts for all of them.

**The Concept:** "You can analyze the past but you have to design the future - or else you will just fall into it."

**Prompting application:**
> "Shift from judgment to design thinking on [SITUATION]. Instead of evaluating what's wrong, design a way forward that accounts for: what everyone needs, what everyone fears, what values must be delivered."

---

### 28. Court of Design

**What it is:** An alternative to adversarial legal/conflict processes. Instead of judging who is right/wrong, a Court of Design brings skilled creative staff to help both parties construct a way forward. No legal standing needed - just agreement to participate.

**How to use it:**
1. Both parties agree to the process (don't need to agree to be bound by outcome)
2. Focus: what way forward would serve both parties' real needs?
3. Apply lateral thinking tools to generate new options
4. Use Six Hats to explore the situation without argument
5. Design solutions rather than declare winners

**Prompting application:**
> "Use a court of design approach on [CONFLICT/DISPUTE]: Not who is right, but what way forward could work for both parties? What are each party's actual needs and fears? Design 3 possible ways forward."

---

### 29. Operacy

**What it is:** The skill of "operating" - getting things done. Alongside literacy and numeracy, de Bono argues we need operacy: thinking for action rather than thinking for description. Design is part of operacy.

**Key distinction:** Information + analysis = knowing. Operacy = doing. Most education produces knowledge but not the skill to act effectively.

**Elements of operacy:**
- Design thinking (putting things together to achieve a purpose)
- Action planning (specific steps to execute)
- Practical judgment (what's feasible, what resources exist)
- Systems thinking (how things interact)

**Prompting application:**
> "Shift to operacy thinking on [TOPIC]: Not what is true or what analysis reveals - but what specific actions would move this forward? Design the operational path."

## SECTION 5: VALUES FRAMEWORK

### 30. Six Value Medals System

**What it is:** A framework for distinguishing 6 different types of value so they can each be specifically assessed rather than jumbled together vaguely. Based on the cognitive limit of 7 items in working memory - 6 medals leaves room for one more if needed.

**Key principle:** Most people see values like "a flock of birds" - vague blur. The Six Value Medals trains you to see specific species - individual, assessable, comparable.

---

### 31. Gold Medal - Human Values

**What it is:** Values that apply directly to human beings as people. Personal, emotional, dignity-related values.

**Includes:** Praise, achievement, pride, significance, recognition, dignity, humiliation (negative), being ignored (negative), self-worth

**Prompting application:**
> "Gold medal assessment: What human values (positive or negative) are at stake for the people involved? Pride, dignity, achievement, recognition, humiliation?"

---

### 32. Silver Medal - Organisational Values

**What it is:** Values related to the mission, purpose, and functioning of the organisation (or family unit, team, etc.)

**Includes:** Profits, market share, brand image, public perception, mission alignment, team cohesion, organisational survival

**Prompting application:**
> "Silver medal assessment: How does this align with or conflict with the organisation's mission, purpose, and key performance measures?"

---

### 33. Steel Medal - Quality Values

**What it is:** How well something does what it is supposed to do. Intrinsic quality - is it what it claims to be? Does it perform its function?

**Includes:** Reliability, durability, precision, consistency, fitness for purpose, technical excellence

**Prompting application:**
> "Steel medal assessment: Does this do what it's supposed to do? How well? What are the quality dimensions?"

---

### 34. Glass Medal - Innovation Values

**What it is:** The value of newness, creativity, innovation itself. Glass = simple material made into many things through creativity. Something may have limited other value but genuine value as a new idea.

**Includes:** Originality, creative merit, novelty, innovation, opening new possibilities

**Prompting application:**
> "Glass medal assessment: What is genuinely new here? What creative or innovation value exists, even if the idea isn't fully practical yet?"

---

### 35. Wood Medal - Ecological/Broader Impact Values

**What it is:** Impact on the surrounding environment in the broadest sense - not just nature, but social ecology, community impact, second-order effects on the system.

**Includes:** Environmental impact, community effects, stakeholder effects beyond direct parties, long-term systemic consequences, social fabric

**Prompting application:**
> "Wood medal assessment: What are the broader ecological effects? What impact on surrounding systems, communities, or the longer-term environment?"

---

### 36. Brass Medal - Perceptual Values

**What it is:** How something will be PERCEIVED, regardless of its actual merit. Brass looks like gold but isn't. Critical insight: worthwhile things are often perceived badly; less worthwhile things sometimes perceived very favorably. Both matter and neither can be ignored.

**Includes:** Public perception, optics, how stakeholders will interpret this, reputational effects, narrative framing

**Prompting application:**
> "Brass medal assessment: How will this actually be perceived by each stakeholder group, regardless of its true merit? What are the optics? What narrative will form around this?"

## SECTION 6: META-THINKING CONCEPTS

### 37. Proto-Truth

**What it is:** A truth we hold as true WHILE WE ARE ACTIVELY TRYING TO CHANGE IT. Solves the trap of "once we have the right answer, we stop thinking." Proto-truth says: hold this as true enough to act on, but keep searching for better.

**The danger it addresses:** The most serious barrier to human progress may be finding "the right answer" and stopping. Proto-truth prevents premature closure.

**Prompting application:**
> "Treat [CURRENT BELIEF/ANSWER] as a proto-truth: true enough to act on, but not so fixed that we've stopped looking. What better version might exist? What would challenge or improve this proto-truth?"

---

### 38. Confliction and De-confliction

**What it is:** Terms de Bono invented for conflict resolution. Confliction = the gradual build-up of factors that create conflict before it becomes visible. De-confliction = identifying and removing those factors.

**Two types of conflict:**
- **Bullying**: One party oppressing another for unjust gain. Requires making consequences uncomfortable.
- **Sillying**: Conflict for no real reason - usually leaders gain importance from the conflict and have no incentive to end it. Key insight: if leaders can be given importance INDEPENDENT of the conflict, the conflict ends.

**The design approach to conflict:**
1. Identify the confliction factors (what built this up?)
2. Understand needs, fears, and greed on BOTH sides
3. Design a way forward that accounts for all parties
4. Use OPV to genuinely understand each position
5. Apply Six Hats to explore without argument

**Prompting application:**
> "Apply de-confliction thinking to [CONFLICT]: What are the confliction factors that built this up? What type of conflict is this (bullying or sillying)? What are each party's real needs and fears? Design three possible ways forward."

---

### 39. Directed Attention vs. Pulled Attention

**What it is:** A core perceptual principle. Attention is either "pulled" by something unusual or striking, or "directed" deliberately by a framework. Pulled attention misses most of what's there. Directed attention gives comprehensive coverage.

**The explorer analogy:** An explorer returns from an island and reports only a smoking volcano and a flightless bird - the only things that "pulled" his attention. Sent back with instructions to look north, then east, then south, then west, he returns with a complete picture.

**Key insight:** All the CoRT tools (PMI, CAF, C&S, etc.) are mechanisms for converting pulled attention into directed attention.

**Prompting application:**
> "Use directed attention (not just pulled attention) on [SITUATION]: Don't just look at what stands out. Deliberately scan: positive aspects, negative aspects, human factors, systemic factors, time horizons, stakeholders. What are you seeing when you direct attention to each sector?"

### Combination Templates for AI Prompting

**For generating creative solutions:**

**For exploring a difficult decision:**

> White Hat - What do we actually know?
> Red Hat - What does gut instinct say?
> Black Hat - What could go wrong?
> Yellow Hat - What value is possible?
> Green Hat - What alternatives haven't we considered?
> Blue Hat - Given all of the above, what is the conclusion and next step?"

**For a thorough situation analysis:**
> "Run the full CoRT toolkit on [SITUATION]:

## APPENDIX: DE BONO'S KEY PRINCIPLES SUMMARIZED

1. **Excellent but not enough**: Our existing thinking (logic, argument, critical thinking) is excellent for what it does - but inadequate for creativity, design, and perception.

2. **Perception over logic**: 90% of thinking errors are errors of perception, not logic (David Perkins research). No amount of excellent logic fixes faulty starting perceptions.

3. **Logic from wrong perception**: Logic applied to wrong perception produces confident wrong conclusions. Perception must come before logic.

4. **The asymmetry principle**: What is obvious in hindsight is NOT reachable by logic in foresight. This is the logical basis for why creativity tools work.

5. **Movement over judgment**: For creativity, "where can I move from here?" is more valuable than "is this right or wrong?"

6. **Design over judgment**: For human problems, designing a way forward is more useful than identifying who is right/wrong.

7. **Possibility over certainty**: The obsession with "right answers" stops thinking. Possibilities must be kept alive.

8. **Parallel over adversarial**: Exploring together beats arguing. Six Hats replaces argument for exploration.

9. **Directed over pulled attention**: Frameworks for directing attention produce better thinking than waiting for what catches our eye.

10. **Values as the purpose of thinking**: Thinking without values is pointless. Values without thinking are dangerous. The purpose of better thinking is to deliver better values.
