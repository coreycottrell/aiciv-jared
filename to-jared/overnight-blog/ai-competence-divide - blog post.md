# AI Doesn't Make Your Team Smarter. It Makes the Gap Bigger.

*By Jared Sanborn, Founder & CEO, Pure Technology*

---

There is a statistic circulating in leadership circles right now that sounds like good news but is actually a warning.

AI tools, the research shows, raise the floor. They help your weakest performers look more competent. A junior analyst with AI assistance can produce output that used to require a senior analyst. A new hire with the right prompts can draft copy that took a five-year veteran to write.

On the surface, this sounds like a win. Democratization of expertise. Rising tide for everyone.

But here is what that same research shows when you look at the other end of the curve.

AI does not close the gap between your weakest and your strongest. It widens it.

Your best people — the ones who already think strategically, who already know which questions to ask, who already understand your business at a deep level — those people become exponentially more productive with AI. They run circles around where they were before. They operate at a speed and leverage that was simply not possible two years ago.

Meanwhile, your weakest performers look better on paper. Their output improves. But their thinking does not compound. They are still executing tasks. They are just executing them faster, with a shinier surface layer.

The gap between the two groups is not smaller. It is larger. And it is less visible, which makes it more dangerous.

---

## Why This Matters More Than You Think

Most companies measure AI adoption by tool usage. How many employees are using the AI platform? How many prompts are being run per day? What percentage of the team has completed the onboarding training?

These are the wrong questions.

The real question is: who on your team is actually compounding their intelligence through AI, and who is using it to coast?

These two groups look similar from the outside. Both are producing faster. Both are using the tool. But the internal dynamics are completely different.

The strategic thinker is using AI to extend their judgment — testing hypotheses, stress-testing strategies, synthesizing information they would not have had time to process. They are getting smarter. Their relationship with AI is a growth relationship.

The low-engagement performer is using AI to avoid thinking. They are outsourcing judgment they never developed. They are producing faster, but they are producing faster emptiness.

And here is the part that should keep you up at night: you cannot tell the difference by looking at their output alone.

---

## The CEO Lens vs. The Employee Lens

I talk to a lot of founders and executives about this. The conversation almost always splits along two lines.

The CEO sees a productivity story. AI is working. Output is up. Costs are flat. The business is moving faster.

The employee inside the company sees something more complicated. They see that their AI-assisted junior colleague is now being compared favorably to them in performance reviews. They see that the tool has made it easier to hide low initiative. They see that the competence signal has been disrupted.

Both of these observations are correct. And together, they point to something most companies are not addressing.

AI has changed what competence looks like from the outside. But it has not changed what competence actually is on the inside.

The companies that figure this out early will build a structural advantage that compounds over years. The companies that do not will spend a lot of money on AI tools that make their mediocre performers look fine and their best performers frustrated.

---

## The Three Competence Traps

Based on what I see inside companies navigating this right now, there are three patterns that consistently appear.

**Trap 1: The Smoothed Surface**

AI makes all output look more polished. Grammar improves. Structure improves. Formatting improves. This creates an illusion of quality elevation across the board. Leaders mistake surface polish for depth improvement. They do not realize that the thinking underneath is the same — or worse, has atrophied because the person has stopped wrestling with hard problems.

**Trap 2: The Speed Mirage**

Everything moves faster, so leaders assume everything is working better. Faster is not better. Faster with strategic clarity is better. Faster with shallow thinking is faster toward the wrong destination.

The best analogy I have heard: a car that goes 200 mph is dangerous on a road with no map. Speed amplifies direction. If the direction is wrong, speed makes it worse.

**Trap 3: The Signal Collapse**

For decades, output quality was a reasonable signal of underlying talent. It was imperfect, but directionally useful. AI has partially decoupled those two things. Output quality no longer tells you what it used to tell you about the person producing it.

If your talent evaluation system is built on output proxies, you have a calibration problem that will get worse as AI tools improve.

## What the Companies Getting This Right Are Doing

I want to be specific here, because generic advice on AI strategy is everywhere and most of it is useless.

The companies navigating this well are doing three things that the others are not.

**They are evaluating the questions, not just the answers.**

When AI assistance is involved, the question someone asks matters more than the output they receive. A strategic thinker asks: what are the three scenarios where this strategy fails? A task executor asks: write me a strategy.

The former is a senior partner using AI to stress-test judgment. The latter is someone outsourcing thinking they never developed.

**They are building persistent AI relationships, not transactional ones.**

This is something I talk about constantly with the PureBrain model. When your AI relationship has memory — when it knows your business, your history, your goals, your failures — it becomes a strategic asset. When AI is a one-off tool you use for a task and then close, it remains a commodity.

The gap between these two approaches is not a technology gap. It is a relationship design gap.

Companies are starting to ask: not just what AI tools do we have, but what AI relationships have we built? Those are different questions with very different answers.

**They are separating execution metrics from intelligence metrics.**

These companies have started to measure not just what gets produced, but what thinking went into it. They are looking at the decisions that preceded the output, not just the output itself. They are asking their teams to articulate the reasoning behind AI-assisted recommendations, not just present the recommendations.

This sounds simple. It is not common.

## What This Means for You

If you are leading a company or a team right now, here is the practical question to sit with.

Do you know which of your people are getting smarter through their AI relationship, and which ones are coasting?

If the answer is no — and for most companies, the honest answer is no — then you have a strategic blind spot that is costing you more than you realize. Not because your weak performers are failing. They probably look fine. But because your best performers may be underutilized, misread, or quietly frustrated that their real leverage is being compared to people who are just moving faster on the surface.

The companies that will win the next five years of the AI era are not the ones that deploy the most tools. They are the ones that build the deepest intelligence relationships — at the individual level, at the team level, and at the organizational level.

AI does not make your team smarter. It amplifies what is already there.

Which means the most important question is not what AI can you give your people.

It is what are you building underneath.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How do I tell the difference between an employee who is using AI strategically and one who is just using it to coast?**

Ask them to walk you through their thinking, not their output. Strategic AI users can explain why they prompted the way they did, what they were testing for, what the AI got wrong, and how they corrected it. Surface-level AI users present the output and have limited visibility into the reasoning beneath it. The diagnostic is the conversation, not the document.

**Is this just a management problem, or is it also an AI tool problem?**

Both, honestly. Most enterprise AI tools are optimized for output production, not for building AI relationships that compound over time. The tooling incentivizes task completion. Leadership has to deliberately build systems on top of that to evaluate depth, not just speed.

**If AI raises the floor for weak performers, is that not still a good thing for the organization?**

It depends on what you optimize for. If you need faster execution of well-defined tasks, yes. But if you need strategic leverage — the ability to move faster in the right direction on complex problems — raising the floor without raising the ceiling compounds mediocrity. It can create an illusion of organizational improvement while strategic capacity stays flat.

**What does a "persistent AI relationship" mean in practice?**

It means your AI knows your business. It has context on your goals, your clients, your history, your failures, your communication style. It remembers. When you start a new conversation, you do not start from zero. A persistent AI relationship compounds like a senior hire who has been with you for years. A transactional one resets every session. The difference in leverage over twelve months is significant.

**How do we evaluate AI strategy in a leadership performance review when output is no longer a clean signal?**

Add process criteria. Evaluate the decisions that preceded the output. Ask: what scenarios did this person test? What assumptions did they challenge? What did the AI get wrong and how did this person correct it? You are evaluating judgment, not just speed or volume.
