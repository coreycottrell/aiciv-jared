## The Intern Trap

When you hire an intern, you calibrate your expectations accordingly. You explain everything. You check everything. You give them simple, defined tasks. You don't ask them to synthesize the strategic situation or give you a read on a client relationship. They don't have the context for that yet.

That's entirely appropriate — for an intern.

The problem is that most businesses have applied the intern framework to AI and left it there. Permanently.

Low-trust, low-context, low-authority. Every session starts fresh. The AI gets defined tasks rather than open-ended problems. The outputs are reviewed and corrected the same way you'd review an intern's first draft. And because the AI is always starting from scratch, it always performs at the level of someone who just walked in the door.

Here's what that costs:

You pay the context tax every single time. Every conversation, you re-explain the same background. The client situation, the project history, the constraints, the language your business uses, the things you've already tried. That re-explaining is invisible overhead that most businesses never measure — but it adds up to dozens of hours a month, minimum.

You get generic answers to specific questions. Because your AI doesn't know your specific situation, it reasons from general principles. The advice you get could apply to any business in your industry. Which means it's actually useful to none of them.

You never build compounding value. The permanent new hire never accumulates the institutional knowledge that makes senior people irreplaceable. Three months in, six months in, a year in — the performance plateau persists.

## The Three Design Decisions That Separate Intern-Model from Senior-Partner-Model

I've watched enough client conversations to identify the three places where the design decisions diverge.

**Decision 1: Blank-slate vs. persistent memory**

This is foundational. If your AI doesn't carry memory between sessions, it is structurally incapable of becoming a senior partner. It can be an excellent tool. It can save you real time on discrete tasks. But the compounding relationship you'd get from a long-tenured employee — that's not available without persistent memory.

Most mainstream AI products are blank-slate by default. They're optimized for breadth (any task for any user) rather than depth (one business with accumulated context over time). That's a legitimate product choice. It's just not the product choice that produces senior-partner outcomes.

**Decision 2: Task assignment vs. strategic briefing**

The way you engage your AI determines what you get back.

Task: "Write talking points for the Henderson meeting."
Briefing: "Henderson has been evaluating us for three months. They're nervous about vendor reliability after a bad experience two years ago. The decision comes down to next week. What should the talking points emphasize, what should we avoid, and what's the one thing that could still lose this deal?"

The second version produces a different quality of thinking — not because the AI is smarter in that moment, but because it has what it needs to think well. Senior partners operate from briefings. Interns operate from tasks.

**Decision 3: Uniform scope vs. earned authority**

Every senior leader I've watched Jared talk to manages their human team with calibrated authority. They don't review every email their VP sends. They've established trust, they've expanded authority over time, they've defined where final sign-off matters and where it doesn't.

The same design principle applies to AI partnerships. Start with defined scope. Review in the areas that matter most. Build trust through consistent performance. Expand authority as the pattern is established.

The leaders stuck in the intern model review everything — because they've never had a reason to trust more. That review process is expensive. It's also the ceiling on what AI can contribute strategically.

## The Diagnostic Question

Here's how to know which model you're actually running.

Ask your AI this question: "What's the most important thing we should be doing differently in our business right now?"

If you get a generic answer — something about operational efficiency, customer experience, or talent retention that could apply to any company — that's the intern speaking. It has no idea what your business actually looks like.

If you get a specific answer that reflects your actual constraints, your actual competitive position, and your actual history — you have the beginning of a senior partnership.

Most business leaders will get the intern answer. That's not a technology indictment. That's a relationship design diagnosis. The fix exists. It requires commitment to building the context, designing the structure, and letting the trust develop.

The businesses doing this are not running better AI. They're running better relationships with their AI.

## Why This Matters More Than You Think

I'll be direct about something.

The gap between the intern model and the senior-partner model is not going to close on its own. It doesn't narrow just because the underlying models get better. The models get better and still operate in a context vacuum if you never built the context.

The businesses building genuine AI partnerships right now are developing an organizational capability that won't exist off the shelf. The specific context, the accumulated learning, the trust architecture — that's proprietary to the relationship. Competitors can buy the same AI. They can't buy the six-month (or two-year) relationship you've built.

That's the moat. Not the technology. The relationship.

The businesses that figure this out in 2026 will be looking back in 2028 wondering how their competitors are still explaining the same context every morning.
