I had a conversation last week with a VP of Operations at a mid-sized company. Smart person. They had run four AI pilots in the past 18 months. None of them had scaled.

"It's not that they failed," she told me. "It's that they never really... stuck."

She is not alone. An MIT research report published last year found that 95% of enterprise AI pilots fail to produce measurable business value. Not catastrophic, headline-grabbing failures. Just a quiet failure to become anything real.

I have been thinking about why. And I want to share what I have found, because I think the answer is different from what most people assume.

---

## The problem is not the technology

The instinct is to blame the model. Wrong vendor. Not powerful enough. Needed a better prompt engineer. Try again next quarter.

But Gartner's analysis points somewhere else: the failure is almost always organizational infrastructure, not AI capability. Specifically, two things keep showing up:

**1. Generic tools deployed without adaptation.** Generic AI is genuinely useful for individuals. But an AI that has never been briefed on your business, your clients, your decision-making norms - it produces answers that are technically correct and organizationally useless. There is a real difference between an AI that can answer anything and an AI that understands your specific context.

**2. No one owns the outcome.** The pilots that never scale almost always share this characteristic: they have sponsors but no owners. Someone approved the budget. No one staked their performance review on the result. The research is stark here - companies that partner with specialized AI vendors succeed roughly 67% of the time. Companies that deploy generic tools and measure success by "number of pilots run" succeed about one-third as often.

---

## The Context Tax

There is a third factor I want to name specifically, because I think it is the most underestimated.

Every time someone on your team opens a new AI session, they start from zero. Re-explain the project. Re-establish the background. Re-describe the constraints. Before they get to anything useful, they have spent 15-20 minutes rebuilding context that should have persisted automatically.

I call this the Context Tax. And for individual use, it is manageable. For enterprise deployments, it is quietly devastating. Multiplied across a team, across departments, across months - the cost of re-briefing compounds dramatically.

The organizations that escape Pilot Purgatory - and it is real, nearly two-thirds of companies were stuck there as of mid-2025 - tend to have one thing in common: they treat AI as infrastructure, not a product. They invest in systems where context persists, where the AI actually learns the organization over time, where each interaction builds on the last rather than resetting.

---

## What this means for you

If you have run an AI pilot in the last 18 months and it did not scale, I would push back gently on the instinct to blame the technology. Ask instead:

- Did we measure outcomes (cost, revenue, risk) or activities (pilots launched, employees trained)?
- Did our AI actually know our business - or was it starting from zero every session?
- Did someone own this with their name attached to the result?

The companies getting real value from AI are not necessarily using better models. They are building better relationships with AI. There is a difference.
