HBR introduced a new job title this month: Agent Manager.

Someone responsible for leading, developing, and getting results from AI agents - the same way a human manager leads a team.

By end of 2026, Forrester predicts 20% of organizations will restructure their entire hierarchy around AI agents.

That's not using AI. That's organizational redesign.

Here's what most leaders are missing:

Managing AI agents is a different skill than using AI tools.

The same way managing a finance team is different from using a spreadsheet.

An AI agent's performance depends on three things:

→ Context depth (what it knows about your business)
→ Feedback architecture (how you improve it over time)
→ Hybrid workflow design (where AI leads, where humans must)

Most deployments get none of these right.

They deploy agents that start from scratch every session. No memory. No accumulated context. A permanent new hire who never gets past week one.

The organizations actually seeing results - Salesforce's 74% autonomous case resolution, $60M in pipeline from one deployment - designed against these three problems from the start.

The skill gap is real.

Workday's February research found 50%+ of IT leaders face shortages of people who can actually orchestrate AI agents in production.

Not data scientists. Not prompt engineers. People who understand AI at a relationship level.

That's the competitive divide opening right now.

→ Are you building AI that compounds?
→ Or paying for tools you underuse?
